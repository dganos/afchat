# Occupant Safety and Protective Equipment

This document covers mandatory occupant safety rules and protective equipment requirements across the European Union, including seatbelt laws, child restraint systems, motorcycle helmet regulations, and required emergency carry items. Rules apply to all EU member states unless otherwise noted; individual countries may impose stricter requirements.

---

## Seatbelt Requirements — Front and Rear Seats

EU Directive 2003/20/EC, which amended Directive 91/671/EEC, made seatbelt use mandatory for all occupants in all seats of motor vehicles equipped with seatbelts. The obligation applies to front and rear passengers equally.

**Front seats.** Every driver and every front-seat passenger must wear a seatbelt at all times the vehicle is in motion on any public road, including motorways, national roads, and urban streets. There is no minimum speed threshold below which the requirement is waived.

**Rear seats.** Rear passengers are also required by EU law to wear seatbelts where fitted. Vehicles first registered after 1 January 1997 must have seatbelts at all outboard rear seating positions; vehicles first registered after 1 October 1999 must also provide a belt at the centre rear seat if three rear seats are present.

**Exemptions.** Recognised exemptions under EU law include: holders of a medical exemption certificate issued by a registered physician; reversing a vehicle; taxi drivers when carrying a fare-paying passenger; delivery drivers making frequent stops within a delivery zone (some member states limit this exemption to speeds below 40 km/h); and emergency service personnel in specified operational contexts. Exemption certificates must be carried and available for inspection.

**Driver responsibility.** The driver is legally responsible for ensuring that passengers under 14 years of age are properly restrained. For passengers aged 14 and over, the legal obligation falls on the individual occupant, though the driver may also bear secondary liability under national law.

## Child Restraint Systems — General Framework

EU rules require all children to be seated in an appropriate child restraint system (CRS) until they reach **135 cm in height OR 12 years of age** — whichever milestone comes first. Once a child meets either threshold, they may use the standard adult seatbelt without a child restraint, provided they can be properly positioned by it.

Child restraint systems sold in the EU must comply with one of two recognised technical standards:

- **ECE Regulation 44/04 (R44/04):** The older weight-based classification system, grouping children by mass: Group 0 (up to 10 kg), Group 0+ (up to 13 kg), Group I (9–18 kg), Group II (15–25 kg), and Group III (22–36 kg). R44/04 seats continue to be sold and used legally throughout the EU, though R44/04 approval for new seat designs was phased out in favour of i-Size.
- **ECE Regulation 129 (i-Size / R129):** The newer height-based system introduced in 2013. Seats are classified by child height range. i-Size mandates rearward-facing use for all children up to at least 100 cm tall (approximately 15 months of age), stricter side-impact testing, and ISOFIX attachment as standard.

It is illegal to use a child restraint that does not bear a valid ECE approval label (an orange or blue label with the "E" mark and regulation number). Aftermarket seat adaptors not bearing ECE approval are prohibited.

## Rearward-Facing Child Seats and Airbag Deactivation

When a rearward-facing child restraint is installed in a **front passenger seat equipped with an active frontal airbag**, the airbag **must be deactivated** before the journey begins. This is a binding requirement under EU law (reinforced by UNECE Regulation 16 requirements integrated into EU homologation standards).

The rationale is that a frontal airbag deploying at between 200 km/h and 300 km/h toward a rearward-facing seat would strike the back of the child seat directly, producing forces that can cause fatal or severe head and neck injuries to an infant.

**Deactivation methods.** Most modern vehicles equipped with front-seat airbags and intended for child-seat use provide either:
1. A key-operated airbag deactivation switch (typically located in the glove box or dashboard side panel), or
2. An automatic sensor that detects occupant weight and/or seat presence and disables the airbag when a child seat is detected.

If neither method is available, the rearward-facing child restraint must be placed in a rear seat only. Placing a rearward-facing seat in front of an active airbag without deactivation is a criminal offence in all EU member states and is treated as an aggravated child endangerment violation in several countries, carrying enhanced penalties.

**Airbag-off indicator.** EU vehicle construction rules require that when a frontal airbag has been manually or automatically deactivated, a clearly visible warning light must remain illuminated on the dashboard throughout the journey to confirm the deactivated state.

## ISOFIX and Tether Anchoring

ISOFIX is the ISO 13216 standard rigid-bar attachment system for child restraints. Under EU type-approval Regulation (EC) No 661/2009, all new passenger cars approved from 1 November 2014 must be fitted with ISOFIX anchorage points at the two outboard rear seating positions as standard equipment.

i-Size (R129) seats exclusively use ISOFIX plus a top-tether strap or a support leg for anti-rotation. R44/04 seats may use ISOFIX or a vehicle seatbelt to attach, depending on the specific seat's approval. Using an ISOFIX seat in a vehicle without ISOFIX anchorages, or using a seatbelt-only seat in an ISOFIX-only position without appropriate adaptation, is non-compliant.

The top-tether anchor point (a hook point above the rear seat, typically integrated into the parcel shelf or seatback) is required in all new cars from 2014 alongside the ISOFIX bars.

## Motorcycle Helmet Requirements

All riders and passengers on motorcycles, mopeds (L-category vehicles), and motorised two- and three-wheelers must wear a crash helmet in all EU member states. This requirement is absolute: there are no exemptions based on age, vehicle engine size, or journey distance.

**Approved standards.** Helmets must meet one of the following ECE approval standards to be legally worn on EU public roads:

- **ECE 22.05:** The fifth revision of UNECE Regulation 22, which was the universal minimum standard across all EU member states for decades. Helmets bearing the ECE 22.05 approval label (a white circle with the letter "E" and a country code, plus the number 22.05) remain legal throughout the EU.
- **ECE 22.06:** The sixth revision, adopted by UNECE in 2020 and implemented in EU member states on a rolling basis. ECE 22.06 introduces more demanding impact attenuation tests, oblique impact testing (to assess rotational acceleration — linked to diffuse axonal injury), chin bar testing for full-face helmets, and enhanced retention system tests. From **January 2024**, new helmet models submitted for type approval in the EU must meet ECE 22.06; ECE 22.05 helmets already type-approved and on the market may continue to be sold and used.

**Helmet types.** Open-face (Type J), full-face (Type P), and modular/flip-front helmets are all permissible provided they bear valid ECE approval. Novelty helmets, half-shell helmets that do not cover the chin, and any helmet lacking an ECE label are illegal for road use throughout the EU.

**Chin strap.** The helmet must be properly fastened with the chin strap secured at all times when the motorcycle is in motion. Riding with an unfastened helmet chin strap is treated as equivalent to not wearing a helmet.

**Passengers.** A motorcycle passenger is subject to the same helmet requirements as the rider. A rider is legally responsible for ensuring a passenger under the age of legal majority is wearing a compliant helmet before the vehicle is moved.

## High-Visibility Reflective Vest Requirements

EU law, reinforced by national highway codes, requires that occupants of broken-down or disabled vehicles who exit onto the carriageway or hard shoulder of a **motorway (autoroute/Autobahn/autostrada) or dual carriageway** wear a high-visibility reflective vest (also called a hi-vis vest or gilet jaune in French usage).

**Standards.** Vests must comply with **EN ISO 20471** (Class 1, 2, or 3) or the earlier **EN 471** standard. Class 2 (at minimum) is required in several EU member states; Class 3 provides the highest retroreflective and fluorescent area.

**Storage location.** The vest must be stored inside the vehicle cabin — typically in the glove box or under/behind a front seat — so that it is accessible without the occupant first exiting the vehicle. A vest stored in the boot/trunk does not comply with national rules (e.g., France, Spain, Belgium) because reaching it requires the occupant to expose themselves to traffic before being visible.

**Who must wear it.** Any person who exits a vehicle that has stopped on a motorway or high-speed road due to breakdown, accident, or emergency is required to don the vest before stepping out of the vehicle. This includes all occupants, not just the driver, under the laws of most EU member states.

**Countries mandating carriage.** France, Spain, Belgium, Italy, Portugal, Austria, Romania, Bulgaria, Croatia, Slovakia, Slovenia, Czech Republic, and others require that at least one (in some countries, one per occupant) hi-vis vest be carried in the vehicle at all times. Germany and the Netherlands also require carriage. The EU has encouraged but not uniformly mandated a single vest per occupant across all states.

## Warning Triangle Requirements

All EU member states require a motor vehicle to carry at least one **warning triangle** conforming to ECE Regulation 27 (reflective triangles for use in the event of breakdown or accident). ECE R27-approved triangles bear an orange approval label.

**Placement rules.** On a normal road, the triangle must be placed at least **30 metres** behind the stationary vehicle to warn oncoming traffic. On a motorway or high-speed road, the minimum placement distance increases to **100 metres** behind the vehicle. On a bend or after a crest, the triangle must be placed before the hazard so drivers have adequate warning distance.

**Number of triangles.** Most EU countries require one triangle. Spain requires two triangles (one placed 50 m in front and one 50 m behind the vehicle). Towing a trailer may require the second triangle in some jurisdictions.

The warning triangle must be placed in addition to, not instead of, hazard warning lights, which must be activated immediately upon stopping in a dangerous position.

## First-Aid Kit and Fire Extinguisher Requirements

The EU does not impose a single uniform rule mandating a first-aid kit in private passenger vehicles, but a majority of member states do so at the national level.

**Countries requiring a first-aid kit in private cars:** Austria, Croatia, Czech Republic, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Romania, Serbia (candidate state), Slovakia, Slovenia, and others. The kit must typically conform to DIN 13164 (German standard) or an equivalent national standard and include gloves, bandages, wound dressings, a foil emergency blanket, and CPR face shield at minimum.

**Fire extinguisher.** A portable fire extinguisher is not mandated for private passenger cars in most EU member states, but is required in commercial vehicles, buses, and heavy goods vehicles under **ADR regulations** and national transport law. Some EU countries (e.g., Bulgaria, Greece) recommend or require extinguishers in private vehicles; Norway (EEA member) requires them in private cars.

**Recommended kit contents (DIN 13164:2022):** 2 pairs of disposable gloves, 1 CPR face shield, 4 wound compresses, 2 triangular bandages, 2 elastic bandages, 4 adhesive dressings, 1 foil rescue blanket, and instruction leaflet.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Penalties vary by member state but the following ranges are representative of EU-wide practice.

| Violation | Representative Fine Range (EUR) | Other Consequences |
|---|---|---|
| Driver not wearing seatbelt | €50 – €500 | Points on licence (e.g., 3 pts in France, 3 pts in Spain) |
| Rear passenger (adult) not belted | €50 – €300 | Points possible |
| Child not in correct CRS | €150 – €1,500 | Points; possible vehicle immobilisation |
| Rearward-facing CRS with active airbag | €300 – €2,000 | Criminal child endangerment charge in severe cases |
| Motorcycle without ECE-approved helmet | €100 – €1,500 | Licence points; confiscation of helmet |
| No hi-vis vest on motorway | €90 – €500 | |
| No warning triangle | €50 – €300 | |
| No first-aid kit (where required) | €25 – €150 | |

**Repeat offences** and violations detected in the context of an accident in which injuries occurred are treated as aggravating factors, typically doubling or trebling the base fine, and may result in licence suspension. Driving while children are unrestrained is classified as a child safety offence in France, Germany, and Spain, carrying mandatory court appearances for serious or repeat cases.

---

## Summary of Key Thresholds

- **Seatbelts:** mandatory for all seats, all occupants, no speed threshold.
- **Child restraints:** required until **135 cm tall OR 12 years old** (whichever comes first).
- **Rearward-facing limit (i-Size):** mandatory rearward-facing until child reaches **100 cm**.
- **Airbag deactivation:** mandatory when rearward-facing seat is in a front seat with active airbag.
- **Motorcycle helmets:** ECE 22.05 (existing stock) or **ECE 22.06** (new approvals from January 2024).
- **Warning triangle placement:** minimum **30 m** on ordinary roads; minimum **100 m** on motorways.
- **Hi-vis vest storage:** inside cabin, not in boot.
