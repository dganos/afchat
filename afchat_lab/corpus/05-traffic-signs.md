# Traffic Signs and Signals

## Overview and Legal Framework

The Vienna Convention on Road Signs and Signals, concluded on 8 November 1968 and entering into force on 6 June 1978, established the international framework for standardising road signs across contracting states. As of 2024, over 70 countries have ratified or acceded to the Convention, including all EU member states. The Convention defines eight principal sign categories, specifies the geometric shapes and colour schemes assigned to each, and sets minimum size requirements. Amendments adopted in 1995 and 2003 extended the system to cover environmental zones, electronic variable message signs, and additional regulatory markings. The Convention does not override national law but requires signatories to align their sign systems with the agreed standards within a reasonable transitional period.

## Sign Categories, Shapes, and Colours

The Vienna Convention organises road signs into eight categories, each with a prescribed geometry and dominant colour scheme.

| Category | Shape | Background colour | Border / symbol colour | Primary function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A — Danger/Warning | Equilateral triangle, point up | White or yellow | Red border, black symbol | Warn of hazard ahead |
| B — Priority | Inverted triangle (give way) / Octagon (stop) / Yellow diamond (priority road) | White / Red+White / Yellow | Red / Red+White / White | Establish right-of-way hierarchy |
| C — Prohibitory/Restrictive | Circle | White | Red border, black symbol | Forbid or restrict a movement |
| D — Mandatory | Circle | Blue | White symbol | Require a specific action |
| E — Information/Direction | Rectangle or square | Blue (motorway), green (primary road), white (local) | White or black symbol | Guide, inform, identify roads |
| F — Service | Rectangle | Blue | White symbol | Indicate service facilities |
| G — Additional panels | Rectangle | White | Black text/symbol | Qualify the sign above |
| H — Road works | Similar to A but orange background in some states | Orange or yellow | Black symbol | Temporary hazard from works |

## Danger and Warning Signs (Category A)

Danger signs are equilateral triangles with the apex pointing upward, a red border at least one-tenth of the side length wide, and a black or dark-grey symbol on a white (or occasionally yellow) background. The minimum side length for signs on roads with a speed limit above 60 km/h is 900 mm; on other roads 600 mm is permitted. Signs are placed 150–250 m before the hazard on high-speed roads and 50–100 m before the hazard in built-up areas, with a distance panel indicating the exact gap when it differs from the standard.

Common examples include: A1 (dangerous bend, left or right), A6 (slippery road — a car with wavy lines beneath it), A14 (pedestrians on roadway), A15 (children), A16 (cyclists), A17 (road narrows), A22 (traffic signals ahead), and A29 (animals crossing). The Convention permits member states to use a yellow background instead of white for improved daytime visibility in high-sunshine regions; the red border and black symbol remain mandatory regardless of background choice.

## Priority Signs (Category B)

Priority signs establish the right-of-way hierarchy at junctions and along roads. The Vienna Convention specifies four distinct signs in this category, each with a unique shape to ensure recognition even when obscured by snow or dirt:

**B1 — Give Way**: An inverted equilateral triangle (apex pointing downward) with a red border and white interior. No text is required; the shape alone is internationally recognised. Drivers must yield to all traffic on the road they are entering.

**B2 — Stop**: A regular octagon with a red background and the word "STOP" in white letters, plus a white border. The octagon has eight equal sides. Drivers must make a complete stop at the stop line (or edge of the carriageway if no line is marked) before proceeding, regardless of visibility.

**B3 — Priority Road**: A yellow square (rotated 45° to appear as a diamond) with a white border. It indicates that traffic on the road has right of way over traffic on intersecting roads. The sign is repeated at intervals on long priority roads.

**B4 — End of Priority Road**: The same yellow diamond with a black diagonal band overlaid, signalling that the priority road designation has ended.

## Prohibitory and Restrictive Signs (Category C)

Prohibitory signs are circles with a white or light-coloured background, a red border, and a black symbol or numeral. The red border width is typically one-seventh of the sign diameter. Restriction signs use the same circle format but may indicate limits rather than outright prohibitions.

Key examples:

- **C1 — No Entry**: A circle with a solid red background and a horizontal white rectangle (the "no entry bar") across the centre. Unlike other Category C signs, the background is entirely red, not white. No additional symbol is needed.
- **C3a — No Overtaking**: White circle, red border, two cars side by side (red car overtaking black car), with an X or diagonal bar.
- **C6 — Speed Limit**: White circle, red border, black numeral stating the maximum speed in km/h (e.g., "50", "70", "130"). The numeral occupies the central field with no additional symbol.
- **C7 — End of Speed Limit**: White circle with a black numeral crossed by one or more diagonal black stripes, or optionally by a grey band, indicating the specific limit has been lifted.
- **C14 — No U-Turn**: White circle, red border, black curved arrow with a red diagonal bar.
- **C22 — No Stopping**: White circle, red border, a red diagonal bar from lower-left to upper-right and a second bar from lower-right to upper-left forming an X (in some national variants a single diagonal on a yellow background).

## Mandatory Signs (Category D)

Mandatory signs are circles with a blue background and a white symbol. They impose a positive obligation on the driver, such as the direction to follow, the minimum speed to maintain, or the track to use. The blue background on a circle is the universal identifier for obligation.

Common examples:

- **D1 — Mandatory straight ahead**: Blue circle, white arrow pointing upward.
- **D2 — Mandatory turn right/left**: Blue circle, white arrow bent in the required direction.
- **D4a — Mandatory cycle track**: Blue circle, white bicycle symbol. Cyclists must use it; motor vehicles are excluded.
- **D7 — Minimum speed**: Blue circle, white numeral (e.g., "30") — the inverse of the red-border speed limit.
- **D9 — End of mandatory minimum speed**: Blue circle with numeral and a diagonal red or grey bar.
- **D10 — Snow chains required**: Blue circle, white symbol of a tyre with a chain.

Mandatory signs remain in force until a sign explicitly cancelling them is displayed, or until the road conditions that triggered them (e.g., a compulsory direction at a junction) have been passed.

## Information and Direction Signs (Category E)

Information signs are rectangles (occasionally squares). Their background colour codes the road type:
- **Blue background**: Motorway (autoroute, Autobahn) signs. Motorway begins with a blue rectangle showing a stylised motorway symbol (car on a divided road) and ends with the same symbol crossed out.
- **Green background**: Primary or national roads.
- **White background**: Local or minor roads.
- **Brown background**: Tourist or leisure destinations.

Direction signs indicate distances in kilometres. Name signs at locality boundaries show the place name in black on a white background (entry) or a white name on a white rectangle with a diagonal black stripe (exit from locality, indicating that speed limits and other urban rules no longer apply).

The European route numbering system uses a green rectangle with a white "E" prefix and numeral (e.g., E40). National route numbers appear in different colours per country but within the framework of Category E.

## Traffic Light Sequences and Meanings

Traffic lights in Vienna Convention countries follow a standardised three-colour sequence. Lights are displayed vertically (red at top, amber in middle, green at bottom) or horizontally in constrained locations.

**Standard sequence for vehicles:**
1. **Red**: Stop behind the stop line. Proceeding is prohibited.
2. **Red + Amber simultaneously**: Prepare to move; the signal is about to change to green. Do not proceed yet.
3. **Green**: Proceed if the way is clear.
4. **Amber alone**: Stop if it is safe to do so; the signal is about to change to red. Proceeding through amber is permitted only when stopping safely is not possible.

**Amber flashing light**: At junctions during low-traffic hours, a single flashing amber light means proceed with caution; drivers must give way as at an uncontrolled junction.

**Red flashing light**: Signals a mandatory stop (used at level crossings, drawbridges, and fire station exits). Two alternating red flashing lights at a level crossing mean a train is approaching; drivers must stop and must not pass until both lights have extinguished and barriers (if fitted) have fully risen.

**Green arrow signal**: A supplementary green arrow (fixed or illuminated) pointing in a specific direction allows movement in that direction even when the main signal is red, provided drivers give way to crossing pedestrians and other traffic with right of way.

**Pedestrian signals**: Green walking figure = cross; red standing figure = wait. In many jurisdictions a flashing green figure means the pedestrian phase is ending — pedestrians already crossing may continue but new crossings should not begin.

## Road Markings

Road markings are supplementary to signs and traffic lights but carry legal force. The Vienna Convention distinguishes white and yellow markings and assigns specific meanings to each pattern:

**Longitudinal white lines:**
- **Single continuous (solid) white line** in the centre of a two-way road: overtaking and crossing prohibited.
- **Single broken (dashed) white line**: Lane boundary; crossing permitted with care.
- **Double solid white lines**: No crossing for any vehicle; equivalent to a physical barrier.
- **Solid line adjacent to broken line**: The driver on the side of the solid line may not cross; the driver on the broken-line side may cross if safe.

**Transverse white markings:**
- **Stop line** (single solid white line across the lane): Mandatory stop position, used with Stop signs (B2) or red traffic lights.
- **Give way line** (double broken white line across the lane): Yield position, used with Give Way signs (B1).
- **Pedestrian crossing (zebra)**: Multiple parallel white stripes running across the carriageway, perpendicular to the kerb. Pedestrians have priority once on the crossing.
- **Bicycle crossing**: Checkered or two parallel dashed lines indicating a cycle crossing adjacent to a pedestrian crossing.

**Yellow markings:**
- **Yellow kerb markings** (continuous yellow line along the edge): No stopping or parking. A broken yellow kerb line means no parking but stopping to load/unload is permitted.
- **Yellow box junction**: Criss-cross yellow lines painted on the carriageway at junctions; vehicles must not enter the box unless their exit is clear, even if the traffic light is green.
- **Reversible lane arrows**: Yellow arrows indicate lane direction in managed lane systems where direction changes at different times of day.

**Zig-zag white lines** (UK-derived but referenced in Convention annexes): White zig-zag markings alongside pedestrian crossings prohibit parking and overtaking in the zone, preserving sightlines to the crossing.

## Signal Priority Order

When different traffic control elements appear to conflict, the Vienna Convention establishes a strict hierarchy:

1. **Police officer or authorised traffic controller signals** — highest authority. A uniformed officer directing traffic overrides all fixed signs, markings, and traffic lights. Arm raised vertically means stop for all approaching traffic; arm extended horizontally means stop for traffic facing the officer's front and back; waving the arm downward means traffic in front of the officer may proceed.
2. **Traffic light signals** — override fixed signs and road markings. A green light permits movement that a static sign might otherwise restrict.
3. **Fixed road signs** (categories A–H) — override road markings in cases of apparent conflict.
4. **Road markings** — lowest standing in the hierarchy; must be observed when no higher-authority control is present.

This four-tier hierarchy ensures that temporary conditions (accident, road works) can be managed by a controller whose instructions supersede the permanent infrastructure, and that electronic signals can modify the effect of static signs during peak periods or adverse weather.

## Specific Sign Details and Dimensions

The Convention annexes specify minimum retroreflective performance classes for signs. Class RA1 (engineering-grade sheeting) is the minimum permitted; Class RA2 (high-intensity) is required on motorways; Class RA3 (diamond-grade) is recommended for overhead gantry signs. Signs must not be illuminated in a way that causes glare but may be internally lit on motorways and dual carriageways.

**Stop sign dimensions**: The octagon must have equal sides; the minimum inscribed circle diameter is 600 mm on ordinary roads and 750 mm on high-speed roads. The white border is 20 mm wide; the word "STOP" uses a sans-serif capital typeface with a minimum letter height of 80 mm.

**Speed limit sign numerals**: On a 600 mm diameter sign the numeral height is 220 mm; on a 900 mm sign it is 330 mm. A single numeral pair (e.g., "30") must be centred both horizontally and vertically within the red border.

**Give Way sign**: The minimum side of the inverted triangle is 700 mm on ordinary roads; the red border is 40 mm at minimum. No text appears on the sign face under the Convention, although national languages may add "GIVE WAY" or equivalent as a supplementary panel below the sign.

**No Entry sign**: The white horizontal bar is centred at the mid-height of the circle; bar height is one-third of the sign diameter and bar width spans from border to border. The entire background within the red border is red — no white field is visible behind the bar.
