# Vehicle Roadworthiness and Technical Requirements

## Overview

The roadworthiness of vehicles operating on EU public roads is governed primarily by **Directive 2014/45/EU** on periodic technical inspection (PTI) of motor vehicles and their trailers. This directive, which entered into force on 20 May 2014 and required transposition by EU Member States by 20 May 2017, established minimum harmonised standards for PTI frequency, inspection scope, and deficiency classification across the Union. It replaced the earlier Directive 2009/40/EC and introduced a three-tier defect classification system (minor, major, dangerous) with standardised remediation timelines. The directive covers passenger cars (category M1), motorcycles (L category), light commercial vehicles (N1), heavy goods vehicles (N2/N3), buses and coaches (M2/M3), and trailers (O category). Agricultural vehicles and certain specialist machines remain subject to national rules.

---

## Periodic Technical Inspection Schedule ("4-2-2" Rule)

For passenger cars (M1) and light commercial vehicles up to 3.5 t (N1), Directive 2014/45/EU establishes the following baseline inspection cadence, commonly called the **4-2-2 rule**:

- **First inspection:** 4 years after the vehicle's initial registration date
- **Subsequent inspections:** every **2 years** thereafter

Member States may tighten this schedule nationally but may not relax it below the directive minimum.

### Accelerated inspection obligations

Certain categories face stricter schedules under Article 5 of the directive and its Annex II:

| Vehicle category | First PTI | Repeat interval |
|---|---|---|
| Passenger car / light van (M1, N1) | 4 years after first registration | Every 2 years |
| Taxi / hire car / ambulance | 1 year after first registration | Annually |
| Heavy goods vehicle (N2, N3 ≥ 3.5 t) | 1 year after first registration | Annually |
| Bus / coach (M2, M3) | 1 year after first registration | Annually |
| Trailer ≥ 3.5 t (O3, O4) | 1 year after first construction | Annually |
| Light trailer < 750 kg (O1) | National discretion | National discretion |
| Motorcycle / moped (L category) | National discretion (directive minimum: 4 years) | National discretion (directive minimum: 2 years) |

Member States such as Germany (HU – Hauptuntersuchung) and France (Contrôle Technique) implement the 4-2-2 schedule for private cars. The Netherlands (APK) and Sweden (Besiktning) follow equivalent schedules. New vehicles in some Member States receive a grace period: in Germany the first HU falls due 36 months after initial registration for vehicles first registered after 2010, which is stricter than the directive minimum of 48 months.

## What Is Inspected: Core PTI Categories

Annex II of Directive 2014/45/EU specifies the minimum mandatory inspection items, grouped into eight functional areas. Inspectors must classify every defect as **minor** (no immediate action required), **major** (vehicle must be re-inspected within a set deadline, typically 2 months) or **dangerous** (vehicle must be taken off the road immediately or before driving away).

### 1. Braking System

The braking system is among the most critical inspection areas. Inspectors assess:

- Condition and wear of brake pads, shoes, discs, and drums
- Brake pedal travel and foot pressure required
- Hydraulic or pneumatic circuit integrity (no leaks, no sponginess)
- **Service brake efficiency:** minimum deceleration rate of **5.0 m/s²** for category M1 vehicles (corresponding to approximately 50 % of vehicle weight on a roller brake tester)
- **Parking brake efficiency:** capable of holding the vehicle on a gradient of at least **16 % (≈ 9.1°)**
- ABS/ESC/EBD warning lights must not remain illuminated during the test

### 2. Steering and Axles

- Steering play must not exceed **13°** on the steering wheel rim before the front wheels begin to move (roughly equivalent to ≤ 15 mm rim play at the rim edge for a typical 350 mm wheel)
- Condition of tie-rod ends, ball joints, rack gaiters, and column couplings
- Wheel bearing play checked for excessive radial and axial movement
- Chassis and suspension geometry evaluated for corrosion, cracks, and deformation

### 3. Visibility

- Windscreen must have no cracks in the **critical zone A** (the driver's primary forward field of view, typically a 29 cm × 10 cm rectangle directly in front of the driver)
- All mirrors must be present, undamaged, and correctly adjustable
- Windscreen wipers and washer system must function effectively
- Tinting film must not exceed national limits (typically ≥ 70 % light transmittance on front side windows in Germany, ≥ 75 % in France)

### 4. Lights and Electrical Equipment

- Headlights must be aligned within ±0.5 % vertically (i.e., aim must not deviate more than 5 cm per 10 m)
- All mandatory lights must be present and operational: low beam, high beam, position/sidelights, brake lights (minimum 2 rear), rear fog light (minimum 1), reversing light, direction indicators, hazard warning lights, licence plate illumination, and daytime running lights (DRL) where legally required
- Light colour must comply: white/yellow for front, red for rear, amber for indicators
- Condition of wiring looms checked for chafing, corrosion, and insecure routing

### 5. Axles, Wheels, and Tyres

Tyres are scrutinised for:

- **Minimum tread depth: 1.6 mm** across the central three-quarters of the tread width and around the entire circumference (consistent with Regulation (EC) No 661/2009 and national implementing rules)
- Tyre size and load/speed index must match the vehicle registration document and the type-approval (or be an approved fitment equivalent)
- No cuts, bulges, or exposed cords
- Mixing of tyre constructions (radial vs cross-ply) on the same axle is prohibited; radial on rear with cross-ply on front is generally not permitted
- Wheel nuts/bolts must be present and tight; no cracks in wheel rims
- Spare wheel (if carried as a full-size spare) is not legally required to be presented during PTI in most Member States

### 6. Chassis, Body, and General Items

- Underbody corrosion assessed: surface rust is minor; perforations within 30 cm of suspension or steering attachment points are typically classified as major or dangerous
- Fuel system integrity: no leaks, fuel cap retained
- Exhaust system sealed, securely mounted, no severe corrosion near the catalytic converter

### 7. Emissions

Emissions testing is mandatory under Annex II and is cross-referenced with the vehicle's type-approval emission standard (Euro 1 through Euro 6d).

**Petrol/gasoline engines – idle CO test:**

| Euro standard | CO limit at idle (warm engine) |
|---|---|
| Euro 1 (1993) | ≤ 3.5 % vol. |
| Euro 2 (1997) | ≤ 3.5 % vol. |
| Euro 3 (2001) | ≤ 0.30 % vol. (with lambda probe) |
| Euro 4 (2006) | ≤ 0.30 % vol. |
| Euro 5 (2011) | ≤ 0.30 % vol. |
| Euro 6 / 6d (2015 / 2021) | ≤ 0.30 % vol. (lambda 1.00 ± 0.03) |

**Diesel engines – smoke opacity (k-value):**

| Technology | Maximum k-value (m⁻¹) |
|---|---|
| Naturally aspirated diesel | ≤ 2.5 m⁻¹ |
| Turbocharged diesel (Euro 3 and earlier) | ≤ 3.0 m⁻¹ |
| Euro 4 and later diesel (DPF-equipped) | ≤ 1.5 m⁻¹ |

A DPF-equipped vehicle with a removed or bypassed particulate filter will almost invariably exceed the 1.5 m⁻¹ limit and receive a **dangerous** classification in most Member States.

### 8. OBD (On-Board Diagnostics)

From 20 May 2018, Annex II of the directive requires inspectors to check the OBD system for all M1 vehicles registered after 1 January 2001 (when OBD became mandatory under Euro 3 type approval). A **MIL (Malfunction Indicator Lamp)** illuminated at idle during the test is classified as at least a **major defect**; the number and nature of stored fault codes is also recorded.

## Euro Emission Standards

EU type-approval emission standards apply at the point of manufacture and determine the emission thresholds a vehicle must meet when new. The relevant legislative series are Regulation (EC) No 715/2007 (light vehicles) and Regulation (EC) No 595/2009 (heavy-duty). Key milestones for light passenger cars (M1):

| Standard | New type approvals from | New registrations from | NOx limit (petrol, mg/km) | NOx limit (diesel, mg/km) | PM limit (diesel, mg/km) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Euro 1 | July 1992 | January 1993 | 970 | 970 | 140 |
| Euro 2 | January 1997 | January 1997 | 500 | 900 | 100 |
| Euro 3 | January 2000 | January 2001 | 150 | 500 | 50 |
| Euro 4 | January 2005 | January 2006 | 80 | 250 | 25 |
| Euro 5 | September 2009 | January 2011 | 60 | 180 | 5 |
| Euro 6b | September 2014 | September 2015 | 60 | 80 | 4.5 |
| Euro 6d-TEMP | September 2017 | September 2019 | 60 | 80 | 4.5 |
| Euro 6d | January 2020 | January 2021 | 60 | 80 | 4.5 |

Euro 6d introduced the Real Driving Emissions (RDE) test protocol alongside the laboratory WLTP cycle, with a Conformity Factor (CF) of **1.43** for NOx (a tolerance above the type-approval limit allowed in real-world testing, reduced from 2.1 in Euro 6d-TEMP). EU regulations require Euro 7 to take effect for new M1 type approvals from **July 2030**, bringing NOx limits down to **30 mg/km** for both petrol and diesel.

| First-aid kit (DIN 13164 or equivalent) | Required | Required | Required | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Fire extinguisher | Not required | Not required | Not required | Not required | Required | Required | Required |
| Spare bulb kit | Not required | Required | Required | Not required | Not required | Not required | Not required |
| Tow rope / tow bar | Not required | Not required | Required | Not required | Not required | Not required | Required |

## Tyre Regulations: Winter, Summer, and All-Season Requirements

### Minimum Tread Depth

Across all EU Member States, the legally enforced minimum tread depth for passenger car tyres is **1.6 mm**, measured at the shallowest groove across the central three-quarters of the tread width and around the full circumference. Most tyre manufacturers and safety organisations (including TÜV and DEKRA) recommend replacement at **3 mm** (winter) and **2 mm** (summer) due to the exponential degradation of wet braking performance below those thresholds, but **1.6 mm** is the legal minimum uniformly.

### Winter Tyre Obligations

Winter tyre obligations vary substantially:

| Country | Legal obligation | Period | Min tread depth (winter) | M+S / 3PMSF required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Germany | Situational (snow, ice, slush on road) | No fixed dates | 1.6 mm (legal); 4 mm recommended | M+S marking sufficient (legacy) |
| Austria | Compulsory | 1 Nov – 15 Apr | 4 mm | M+S or 3PMSF |
| Finland | Compulsory (studded allowed) | 1 Nov – 31 Mar | 3 mm | 3PMSF |
| Sweden | Compulsory | 1 Dec – 31 Mar | 3 mm | 3PMSF |
| France | Compulsory (specific mountain zones) | 1 Nov – 31 Mar | 3.5 mm | M+S or 3PMSF |
| Spain | No national obligation | — | 1.6 mm | — |
| Italy | Regional obligation (northern/mountain roads) | 15 Nov – 15 Apr (varies) | 1.6 mm | M+S or chains |

The **Three-Peak Mountain Snowflake (3PMSF)** symbol on a tyre sidewall indicates it has passed an ECE R117 performance test at −7 °C on compacted snow, guaranteeing a minimum 25 % improvement in traction over a reference tyre. The **M+S (Mud and Snow)** designation is a self-certification with no standardised performance test; from **1 October 2024**, Germany no longer accepts new M+S-only tyres (without 3PMSF) as compliant for winter conditions when purchased new.

### Snow Chains

Snow chains are permitted on vehicles fitted with suitable tyres in all EU Member States. Where signs display the **"snow chains compulsory" pictogram** (blue rectangle, chain symbol), chains (or equivalent traction devices such as AutoSock textile covers) are mandatory regardless of tyre type. Maximum recommended speed with chains fitted is generally **50 km/h** across EU countries, though manufacturer instructions may specify lower limits.

## Roadworthiness Certificate and Registration Document Requirements

### Roadworthiness Certificate (PTI Pass Certificate)

Upon successfully passing the PTI, the vehicle owner receives a **roadworthiness certificate** (sometimes referred to as a control card or test report). It must state:

- Vehicle identification number (VIN / chassis number)
- Registration plate number
- Date of the test and name of the testing centre
- Odometer reading at time of test
- Any minor deficiencies noted (to be rectified before next PTI)
- Expiry date of the certificate (i.e., date of next mandatory PTI)

In most Member States the certificate is both issued as a paper document and recorded in a national vehicle database. Germany, France, and Sweden link PTI records to the central vehicle registration database, enabling authorities to verify status via roadside electronic checks.

### Vehicle Registration Document

Under **Directive 1999/37/EC** (as amended by Directive 2003/127/EC), all EU Member States issue registration documents in a harmonised two-part format:

- **Part I (Certificate of Registration Part I):** Must be carried in the vehicle at all times while driving; contains registration plate, VIN, technical data, owner name, and a PTI validity indication in some Member States
- **Part II (Certificate of Registration Part II):** Held by the registered owner (or financing institution); required for transfer of ownership

Loss of Part I can result in a fine: €135 in France, up to €150 in Germany, and up to €400 in Spain for driving without the registration document.

### Sticker / Vignette Systems

Several Member States additionally require a **PTI vignette** affixed to the windscreen:

- **Germany:** A coloured oval TÜV/DEKRA sticker is placed on the rear number plate; the colour rotates on a six-colour cycle and the central month number indicates the month of next test
- **France:** A Contrôle Technique vignette is placed on the lower-left corner of the windscreen; it is valid for 2 years from the test date
- **Austria:** A Pickerl (§57a vignette) is placed on the windscreen; the colour and number encode the year and month of the next inspection

## Defect Classification and Consequences

Directive 2014/45/EU mandates that all PTI results be assigned to one of three defect classes:

| Class | Definition | Required action |
|---|---|---|
| Minor defect | No significant effect on vehicle safety; minor non-compliance | No immediate action; rectify before next PTI |
| Major defect | Affects vehicle safety, road safety, or environmental protection | Vehicle may be used; re-test required within 2 months in most MS |
| Dangerous defect | Constitutes a direct and immediate risk to road safety | Vehicle must not be driven until defect is rectified; immediate re-test required |

A vehicle with a **dangerous defect** result will not receive a valid roadworthiness certificate. In Germany, driving a vehicle with a known dangerous defect and an expired HU (Hauptuntersuchung) sticker voids insurance coverage under § 7 StVZO. In France, a Contrôle Technique result of **Contre-visite obligatoire** (mandatory re-test) for a major defect gives the owner 2 months to rectify faults and present the vehicle for re-inspection; if the re-test is not passed within that window, the registration may be suspended.

## Cross-Border Recognition and Market Surveillance

Under Article 9 of Directive 2014/45/EU, a valid roadworthiness certificate issued in any EU Member State must be recognised by all other Member States. A German vehicle with a valid HU sticker is therefore treated as roadworthy in France, Sweden, or Poland without requiring an additional national test. However, if a vehicle has been **re-registered** in a new Member State, the receiving country's authorities may require a local PTI before issuing new registration plates.

**Commission Regulation (EU) 2019/621** specifies the technical information on items to be tested and the test methods to be applied in PTI, supplementing the directive with granular pass/fail thresholds. Testing centres must be accredited and subject to auditing under national quality assurance schemes; inspectors must hold a qualification recognised by the competent authority of the Member State.
