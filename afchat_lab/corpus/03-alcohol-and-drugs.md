# Alcohol and Drugs While Driving

## Overview and Legal Framework

Driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs is one of the leading causes of road fatalities across the European Union. EU member states are free to set their own legal blood alcohol concentration (BAC) limits under the principle of subsidiarity, meaning there is no single harmonised EU-wide threshold. However, the European Commission and the European Transport Safety Council (ETSC) have long advocated for a uniform general limit of 0.5 g/L (equivalent to 0.05% BAC or 50 mg of alcohol per 100 mL of blood). The majority of EU member states have adopted this 0.5 g/L limit as the standard general threshold for non-professional, experienced drivers. Separate, stricter limits — typically 0.2 g/L or absolute zero — apply to novice drivers, professional drivers (including those holding category C, CE, D, and DE licences), and drivers of vehicles carrying dangerous goods (ADR transport). EU Directive 2006/126/EC on driving licences indirectly reinforces member-state policies on fitness to drive, but does not directly mandate a BAC ceiling.

## Blood Alcohol Concentration: How It Is Measured

Law enforcement officers across the EU use two primary methods to determine a driver's BAC: breathalyser testing and blood testing.

**Breathalyser (evidential breath test):** Roadside screening is conducted using portable breathalyser devices. If a preliminary screen is positive, the driver is typically required to submit to an evidential breath test using an approved, calibrated device (such as the Lion Intoxilyzer or Dräger Alcotest series). Results are expressed in micrograms of alcohol per litre of breath (µg/L). The conversion factor commonly applied is 2,300:1 — meaning 1 g/L blood alcohol is equivalent to approximately 0.435 mg/L in breath (some jurisdictions use a slightly different ratio such as 2,100:1). In France, the statutory breath threshold is 0.25 mg/L breath, corresponding to 0.5 g/L blood.

**Blood test:** If a driver cannot provide a breath sample (e.g., due to a medical condition), or if they challenge a breath result, a venous blood sample is drawn by a qualified medical professional. The sample is analysed by gas chromatography, which is considered the definitive forensic method. Results are expressed in grams of alcohol per litre of blood (g/L) or milligrams per millilitre (mg/mL). A result of 0.5 g/L is equivalent to 0.5 mg/mL or 50 mg/100 mL.

**Urine testing** is used in some jurisdictions as a secondary confirmation method, though it is less reliable for precise BAC determination and is more commonly employed in drug screening.

## Zero-Tolerance Countries

Four EU member states enforce an absolute zero-tolerance policy for all drivers regardless of experience: **Czechia, Hungary, Romania, and Slovakia**. In practical enforcement, "zero tolerance" generally means any detectable BAC above the analytical minimum (typically above 0.0 g/L but with an instrument tolerance of ±0.02 g/L in some jurisdictions). Hungary introduced its zero-tolerance rule in 2008 following a period of lenient enforcement under a prior 0.5 g/L regime. Czechia has maintained a zero BAC limit since 1953, making it one of the world's longest-standing zero-tolerance jurisdictions for drink-driving. Romania adopted zero tolerance in 2013 as part of a national road safety reform package. Slovakia's zero-tolerance policy is codified in Act No. 8/2009 Coll. on Road Traffic.

Croatia and Germany apply zero tolerance specifically to young or novice drivers (under 24 and under 21 years of age, respectively) rather than the general driving population.

## Penalty Structures: Tiered by BAC Band

Most EU member states impose **escalating penalties** based on the severity of the BAC reading, creating distinct penalty bands. The following example is based on the French system, which is broadly representative of the tiered approach used across the continent.

**France (illustrative tiered system):**
- BAC 0.5–0.79 g/L: Administrative offence — fixed fine of €135, automatic loss of 6 points from the 12-point licence, and a 72-hour administrative licence suspension.
- BAC 0.8 g/L or above: Criminal offence — fine of up to €4,500, loss of 6 licence points, and a court-imposed licence suspension of up to 3 years. A prison sentence of up to 2 years may apply.
- BAC 0.8 g/L or above with an aggravating circumstance (e.g., speeding, accident causing injury): Prison sentence of up to 10 years and a fine of up to €150,000.
- Repeat offence (second conviction within 10 years): Compulsory vehicle confiscation, permanent licence revocation, and up to 4 years' imprisonment.

**Germany (illustrative tiered system):**
- BAC 0.3–0.49 g/L with concurrent driving impairment: Fine, 2 penalty points (Punkte), and possible 1–3 month licence ban.
- BAC 0.5–1.09 g/L (first offence): Fine of €500, 2 penalty points, and a 1-month licence ban.
- BAC 0.5–1.09 g/L (second offence within 10 years): Fine of €1,000, 2 penalty points, and a 3-month licence ban.
- BAC 0.5–1.09 g/L (third offence): Fine of €1,500, 2 penalty points, and a 3-month licence ban.
- BAC ≥ 1.1 g/L (Fahruntüchtigkeit — absolute unfitness to drive): Criminal charge under §316 StGB (Road Traffic Act); fine or imprisonment of up to 1 year for first offence; up to 5 years if bodily harm or death results.
- Medical-psychological assessment (MPU, colloquially "Idiotentest") mandatory before licence reinstatement if BAC ≥ 1.6 g/L or repeat offence.

**Sweden:** With a general limit of 0.2 g/L, Sweden treats any reading between 0.2 and 0.99 g/L as a "drink-driving offence" (rattfylleri) — fine and possible licence suspension. Readings of 1.0 g/L or above constitute "gross drink-driving" (grovt rattfylleri) — mandatory imprisonment of at least 1 month, with a maximum of 2 years.

## Drug-Driving: Zero Tolerance and Roadside Testing

EU member states increasingly treat drug-impaired driving (DUID — Driving Under the Influence of Drugs) as a separate and serious offence. Unlike alcohol, where a specific BAC threshold is linked to impairment probability, many EU countries adopt a **zero-tolerance approach to drugs**: the mere presence of a controlled substance in a driver's system — regardless of actual impairment — constitutes an offence.

**Substances covered:** Most national legislation specifically lists: THC (cannabis), cocaine and benzoylecgonine (cocaine metabolite), amphetamine, methamphetamine, MDMA (ecstasy), morphine and 6-monoacetylmorphine (heroin metabolite), and delta-9-THC. EU Directive 2014/85/EU on the periodic testing of professional drivers requires member states to assess fitness to drive, implicitly covering drug use.

**Roadside saliva testing (oral fluid testing):** Most EU member states have authorised roadside oral fluid screening devices. Commonly used devices include the Dräger DrugTest 5000 and the Alere DDS2, which test for multiple substance classes simultaneously. A positive saliva screen typically triggers a confirmatory blood draw, analysed by liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) at a forensic laboratory. In the Netherlands, the threshold for THC in blood is 3.0 µg/L (nanograms per millilitre), established under the 2017 road traffic amendment.

**Prescription medications:** Drivers using benzodiazepines, opioid analgesics, certain antihistamines, or other psychoactive prescription drugs may be liable if their driving is impaired. Several countries (France, Belgium, Germany) attach warning labels (category II and III drugs) to prescriptions affecting driving ability, and drivers may be prosecuted if found impaired regardless of a valid prescription.

## Refusal to Test

Refusing to provide a breath, blood, saliva, or urine sample when lawfully required by a police officer is itself a criminal offence in all EU member states. The consequences of refusal are designed to be at least as severe as the maximum penalty for the highest BAC band:

- **Germany:** Refusal is treated as equivalent to a BAC of ≥ 1.6 g/L — automatic MPU requirement, criminal charges.
- **France:** Refusal to submit to a breath or blood test carries a penalty of €4,500, loss of 6 licence points, and up to 2 years' imprisonment — the same as the highest alcohol band.
- **UK (England/Wales):** Refusal carries a mandatory 12-month disqualification, an unlimited fine, and up to 6 months' imprisonment.
- **Spain:** Refusal is a criminal offence under Article 383 of the Spanish Penal Code, punishable by imprisonment of 6 months to 1 year, or community service.

In all cases, refusal cannot be used to escape prosecution; courts may draw adverse inferences from a refusal.

## Licence Points and Administrative Consequences

Beyond fines and criminal penalties, most EU member states operate a **penalty points system** (or demerit points system) that attaches points to a driver's licence upon conviction for alcohol or drug-driving offences. Accumulating a threshold number of points results in automatic suspension or revocation.

- **France:** 12-point licence. A drink-drive offence above 0.5 g/L removes 6 points. A driver reduced to 0 points loses their licence; novice drivers begin with 6 points on a probationary system.
- **Spain:** 12-point licence (8 points for novices). A drink-drive conviction at 0.5–0.99 g/L removes 4 points; at ≥ 1.0 g/L removes 6 points. Zero-point drivers must retake the theory test and demonstrate sobriety before reinstatement.
- **Italy:** 10-point licence. A BAC of 0.5–0.79 g/L results in a 10-point deduction (i.e., immediate suspension for most drivers). BAC ≥ 0.8 g/L is criminal; ≥ 1.5 g/L triggers mandatory vehicle seizure.
- **Germany:** 8-point Flensburg register (Fahreignungsregister). Drink-driving at 0.5–1.09 g/L adds 2 points; reaching 8 points results in licence revocation. Points are reduced over time if no new offences are committed.
- **Poland:** 24-point licence. Between 0.2–0.5 g/L (civil infraction) incurs a fine of PLN 2,500 and 10 points. Above 0.5 g/L is a criminal offence.

## Aggravating Circumstances

Across the EU, a number of circumstances lead to enhanced penalties when combined with alcohol or drug impairment:

1. **Causing a traffic accident:** Penalties for DUI-related accidents resulting in injury are typically 2–4 times the standard maximum. In France, causing death while DUI carries up to 10 years' imprisonment and a €150,000 fine.
2. **Exceeding the criminal BAC threshold while also speeding:** France and Belgium treat this as an "accumulation of offences" (cumul d'infractions), with each offence penalised independently.
3. **Driving with minors in the vehicle:** France, Spain, and Italy impose enhanced penalties (additional fine, additional licence points deducted) when a child under 15 is a passenger.
4. **Commercial or professional driving context:** A bus driver or HGV driver found above the applicable professional limit faces immediate licence suspension and potential loss of their certificate of professional competence (CPC).
5. **Repeat offending:** A second DUI conviction within 5 years (France), 10 years (Germany), or the lifetime of the licence (Sweden for gross drink-driving) typically doubles the penalty and may trigger compulsory alcohol ignition interlock device (AIID) installation.

## Alcohol Ignition Interlock Devices

An alcohol ignition interlock device (AIID) is a breathalyser connected to the vehicle's ignition system. The driver must provide a clean breath sample (below a preset threshold, typically 0.2 g/L or 0.0 g/L) before the engine will start. Rolling retests — required while driving — prevent a driver from having someone else blow into the device at the start.

**EU adoption:** Sweden has operated a voluntary AIID programme since 1999 and has integrated interlocks into commercial vehicle certification since 2012. France made AIID installation a sentencing option from 2004, and it became mandatory for certain repeat offenders from 2019. Belgium introduced court-ordered AIID as an alternative to licence revocation from 2010. The EU Commission's Road Safety Policy Framework 2021–2030 identifies wider AIID adoption as a key measure toward the Vision Zero goal (zero road deaths by 2050).

**Professional vehicles:** EU Regulation 165/2014 on tachographs references fitness to drive, and several member states require interlocks on buses and coaches following a drink-drive conviction. Sweden mandates interlocks on all publicly-funded transport vehicles irrespective of prior convictions.

## Cross-Border Enforcement and Mutual Recognition

A driver from Germany convicted of drink-driving in France will have the French conviction noted on the EU driving licence record. Under **EU Directive 2015/413/EU** (the Cross-Border Enforcement Directive, CBE), information on traffic offences — including DUI — can be exchanged between member states via the European Vehicle and Driving Licence Information System (EUCARIS). However, direct mutual recognition of licence disqualifications across borders remains incomplete: a disqualification issued in one member state does not automatically prevent driving in another, though many bilateral agreements exist. The UK, post-Brexit, is no longer part of the CBE framework, though it maintains bilateral arrangements with Ireland and some other European states.

Penalties issued to foreign drivers in countries where they are non-residents are pursued via debt collection agencies and mutual assistance requests under Council Directive 2010/24/EU on mutual assistance for the recovery of claims.
