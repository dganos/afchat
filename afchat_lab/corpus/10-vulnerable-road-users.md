# Vulnerable Road Users: Cycling, E-Scooters and Pedestrians

## 1. Overview and Policy Framework

The European Union's road-safety strategy — widely known as **Vision Zero** — aims to reduce road fatalities and serious injuries to near zero by 2050. Within that framework, vulnerable road users (VRUs) receive the highest level of protection: pedestrians, cyclists, e-scooter riders, and motorcyclists together account for roughly 70 % of all road deaths in urban areas according to the European Commission's Road Safety Annual Statistics.

The **EU Road Safety Policy Framework 2021–2030** sets an intermediate target of halving deaths and serious injuries by 2030 compared with the 2019 baseline. Member states are individually responsible for translating EU directives into national law, which means specific rules for helmets, minimum passing distances, and personal mobility devices vary by country. However, the **Safe System approach** underpins all national implementations: roads, vehicles, and speed limits are designed so that a human error does not result in a fatal or life-changing injury. Under this principle, a driver who collides with a pedestrian or cyclist bears a higher burden of proof that they exercised due care — this is sometimes called the **protection principle** or **presumed liability** rule, which is law in several member states including the Netherlands, Belgium, and Germany.

## 2. Cyclist Helmet Rules by Country

Helmet use is the most fragmented area of cycling regulation in the EU. No EU-wide mandatory helmet law exists; each member state decides.

**Countries where helmets are mandatory for all ages:**
- **Spain**: mandatory for all cyclists on interurban roads and outside urban areas (since 2004). Within urban areas, adults may ride without a helmet, but all riders under 16 must wear one at all times, including in towns.
- **Croatia**: mandatory for all cyclists at all times since 2011.
- **Slovakia**: mandatory for cyclists under 15 years old at all times and for all cyclists on roads outside of villages.

**Countries where helmets are mandatory for under-16s on public roads:**
- **Finland**: under 15 years old.
- **Sweden**: under 15 years old (since 2005); breach carries a fine of SEK 500 (approx. €45).
- **Czech Republic**: under 18 years old on roads.
- **Austria**: under 12 years old.

**Countries with no mandatory helmet law for any age group on public roads:**
- France, Germany, Italy, Netherlands, Belgium, Denmark, Poland (as of 2025).

In all countries, helmets worn must meet the **EN 1078** standard (or equivalent) to provide recognised protection. Helmets for children under three years old must comply with **EN 1080**, which has a lower retention-system release force to prevent strangulation.

## 3. Bike Lights, Reflectors, and Night-Riding Requirements

Across EU member states, cycling at night or in conditions of poor visibility without lights is a traffic offence. The standard requirement — consistent across Germany, France, Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, and most other member states — is:

- **White or yellow front light**: visible from at least 150 m in clear conditions; must be fixed to the bicycle (not just the rider's clothing) in most countries.
- **Red rear light**: mandatory, visible from at least 100 m.
- **Red rear reflector**: mandatory at all times (day and night), minimum 20 cm² reflective surface per ECE Regulation 3.
- **Pedal reflectors**: four amber pedal reflectors (two per pedal, front and rear), required in Germany (StVZO §67), France, and numerous other states.
- **Wheel reflectors or tyre reflective inserts**: white reflectors visible from each side; required in Germany and the Netherlands.

In Germany, bicycle lights must comply with **StVZO §67** and must be powered by a dynamo or battery; battery-powered lights must remain functional and switched on whenever visibility is reduced. France requires a **150 m front visibility** minimum and mandates a yellow or orange safety vest (gilet jaune) for cyclists on roads outside of built-up areas at night or in poor visibility.

In the Netherlands, the fine for cycling without lights at night is **€55** per light missing (2024 tariff), making it €110 if both front and rear lights are absent.

## 4. Hand Signals and Traffic Rules for Cyclists

Cyclists must use hand signals to indicate direction changes and stops in all EU member states. The standardised signals (consistent with the 1968 Vienna Convention on Road Traffic, to which all EU states are party) are:

- **Left turn**: left arm extended horizontally.
- **Right turn**: right arm extended horizontally, OR left arm raised at elbow 90 degrees (the latter especially used when it is unsafe to take the right hand off the handlebars).
- **Stopping or slowing**: either arm extended downward at 45 degrees or swung down and back.

In Germany and Austria, cyclists are additionally required to signal before pulling away from a stopped position if other traffic is present. Failure to signal appropriately can result in a cyclist being found partially at fault in a collision, reducing compensation claims under contributory negligence rules.

## 5. Minimum Overtaking Distance for Cyclists

The minimum lateral distance that motor vehicles must maintain when overtaking a cyclist is one of the most safety-critical rules for vulnerable road users.

| Country | Minimum overtaking distance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| France | 1.5 m (urban); 1.0 m (rural) | Introduced 2015; 1.0 m applies where 1.5 m is physically impossible |
| Germany | 1.5 m (urban); 1.5 m (rural) | StVO amendment 2020; same 1.5 m everywhere |
| Spain | 1.5 m | Any road type; mandatory since 2011 |
| Portugal | 1.5 m | Mandatory since 2016 |
| Netherlands | 1.0 m (minimum) | No specific statutory distance but 1 m judicial standard |
| Belgium | 1.0 m (urban); 1.5 m (rural) | Highway Code 2021 |
| Italy | 1.5 m | Codice della Strada, Art. 148 |
| Austria | 1.5 m | StVO amendment 2017 |

In Germany, the **1.5 m rule** introduced by the 2020 StVO reform also applies when overtaking pedestrians and persons using mobility aids. A driver who passes at less than 1.5 m in an urban area may be fined €80; on roads outside built-up areas, the fine is also €80, and a single penalty point is recorded in Flensburg (the German driving licence register).

## 6. E-Scooters and Personal Mobility Devices

Electric scooters (e-scooters) and other personal mobility devices (PMDs) — including hoverboards, self-balancing scooters, and electric unicycles — have been integrated into national road codes primarily after 2018. The EU has not enacted a single harmonised PMD directive; instead, each member state has established its own rules. The following parameters are most commonly regulated.

### Speed and Power Limits

Most EU member states cap e-scooters at **25 km/h** maximum assisted speed, aligning with the EU's L-category vehicle definition for light electric vehicles. Germany permits up to **20 km/h** (§1 eKFV, 2019). France caps shared-rental e-scooters (trottinettes en libre-service) at **20 km/h** in most municipalities, having legislated to allow mayors to lower the limit to **10 km/h** in pedestrian-heavy zones. The motor power limit is generally **500 W** (Germany: 500 W; France: 500 W; Spain: 250 W on pavements, 1000 W off-pavement paths).

### Minimum Age

| Device / Country | Minimum age | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| E-scooter — Germany | 14 years | No licence required |
| E-scooter — France | 12 years (private); 14 years (rental) | Age 12 applies on private shared paths only |
| E-scooter — Spain | 16 years | Municipal bylaws may be stricter |
| E-scooter — Italy | 14 years | Introduced 2020 Highway Code revision |
| E-scooter — Netherlands | 16 years | Trial regulation 2023 |
| E-scooter — Belgium | 16 years | Royal Decree 2019 |
| E-scooter — Sweden | No minimum (shared) | Operators may impose own minimums |
| E-bike (speed pedelec, 45 km/h) — EU typical | 16 years | Moped licence (AM) required in most states |

### Where E-Scooters May Ride

In Germany, e-scooters must use **bicycle infrastructure** (cycle lanes and cycle paths) where available. When no cycle infrastructure exists, they must use the **carriageway** (road). They are **prohibited from pavements** and from pedestrian zones unless local signage permits. They may not use motorways or roads with a speed limit above 50 km/h unless a cycle lane is provided.

In France, e-scooters are prohibited from **pavements** (trottoirs) unless local prefectural order provides an exception; riders caught on pavements face a **€135 fine**. They may use cycle lanes, cycle paths, and roads with a speed limit of up to **50 km/h**. On roads between 50 and 80 km/h, they may ride only if there is a protected cycle lane.

In Spain, e-scooters are classified as **VMP (Vehículo de Movilidad Personal)** and may not be ridden on motorways, highways (vías interurbanas), or on pavements. They are restricted to urban roads with a maximum speed limit of **30 km/h** unless a segregated cycle lane is available.

### Alcohol Limits for E-Scooter Riders

E-scooter riders are subject to drink-driving rules in all EU member states where they are classified as "vehicle" operators. In Germany, the general **0.5 ‰ BAC** limit applies, with a zero-tolerance rule for riders under 21 and for commercial riders. France applies the same **0.5 g/L blood alcohol** limit as for motor vehicles; for riders under 18, the limit is **0.2 g/L** (effectively zero). Spain applies **0.5 g/L** to e-scooter riders (0.3 g/L for new licence holders and professional drivers). In Italy, the limit is **0.5 g/L**, with a zero-tolerance rule for under-21 riders on e-scooters at night.

## 7. Pedestrian Priority and Zebra Crossings

Under the Vienna Convention on Road Traffic (Art. 21), all signatory EU member states must give pedestrians priority at marked pedestrian crossings where no traffic signal is present (i.e., zebra crossings). In practice, implementation and enforcement vary considerably.

**Key rules across EU member states:**

- **France**: since **December 2010** (Code de la Route, Art. R415-11), any driver who fails to yield to a pedestrian who has stepped onto or is clearly about to step onto a zebra crossing faces a **€135 fine** and **6 penalty points** (out of 12 before licence withdrawal).
- **Germany**: under **StVO §26**, pedestrians who have signalled their intent to cross or have begun to cross must be given priority. The fine for failing to yield is **€80** with one Flensburg point; if the pedestrian is endangered, the fine rises to **€100**.
- **Italy**: under the Codice della Strada (Art. 191), vehicles must stop before the stop line whenever a pedestrian is on or visibly about to use a pedestrian crossing. Fines range from €167 to €665.
- **Netherlands**: pedestrians always have priority on zebra crossings; a driver failing to yield faces a **€370 fine** (2024 tariff).
- **Spain**: vehicles must yield to pedestrians who have begun to cross on a zebra. The fine is **€200** with 4 penalty points.

At **signalised crossings**, pedestrians may cross only on the green pedestrian phase. Drivers turning across a pedestrian crossing phase must give way to pedestrians using the green phase even when the vehicle signal is also green.

## 8. School Zones, School Crossings, and Child-Safety Measures

EU member states have implemented a range of measures around schools that go beyond standard pedestrian crossing rules.

**Speed limits**: most member states permit local authorities to impose a **30 km/h zone** (Zone 30 in German; Zone 30 in French) around schools during school hours. In France, **School Streets** (Rues Scolaires) can be closed to motor vehicles entirely during arrival and departure times. Germany allows **Tempo-30 zones** permanent around schools at all times.

**School crossing patrols**: professional or volunteer crossing patrol officers (known as Schulweglotsen in Germany, agents de sécurité scolaire in France) are authorised to stop traffic. Drivers must obey their instructions in the same way as police signals under national road codes.

**Flashing amber lights**: in Spain, schools display flashing amber lights during school hours to indicate a school zone with a **30 km/h** limit. Ignoring the flashing amber incurs a **€200 fine**.

**Bus stops for school transport**: in Germany, school buses (gelbe Schulbusse) that are stationary and loading or unloading children must not be overtaken; vehicles behind must stop. A **hazard light** activation signals a compulsory stop; the fine for overtaking is **€80** with one Flensburg point.

## 9. Bus Lanes and Shared Infrastructure

Cyclists are commonly permitted to use bus lanes in EU cities, though the rule is not uniform.

In **France**, national law (Code de la Route) permits cyclists to use bus lanes by default unless a specific sign (a bus lane panel with a bicycle crossed out) prohibits it. Paris extended this rule city-wide in 2015, and the same policy applies in Lyon, Marseille, and Bordeaux.

In **Germany**, the use of a bus lane by cyclists requires an explicit supplementary sign (Zusatzzeichen) showing a bicycle — without it, cyclists may not use the bus lane. In Berlin, most dedicated bus lanes carry this supplementary sign.

In the **Netherlands**, **fietsstroken** (marked cycle lanes) are distinct from bus lanes; however, where a combined bus-bicycle lane exists (aangeduide busbaan met fiets), cyclists are automatically permitted.

In **Spain** and **Italy**, cyclists may generally use bus lanes in urban areas unless local signage excludes them, following a 2014 reform.

Pedestrians may **not** use bus lanes or cycle lanes as walking paths except at designated crossing points. Cycling on **pedestrian zones** is prohibited unless a sign specifically permits it (e.g., a blue cycling sign with speed limit shown, common in Dutch and German city centres where speeds are capped at **15 km/h**).

## 10. EU Vision Zero and the Protection Principle

The **Safe System** framework underpinning Vision Zero rests on four pillars: **safe road use, safe roads and roadsides, safe speeds, and safe vehicles**. For vulnerable road users, the most consequential policy lever is presumed liability (also called the protection principle), which shifts the burden of proof in civil and criminal proceedings.

**Presumed liability rules by member state:**

- **Netherlands**: since 1990 (Wegenverkeerswet Art. 185), a motorist is automatically liable for injuries to cyclists and pedestrians unless they prove force majeure or that the VRU's behaviour was unforeseeable.
- **Germany**: the StVG (Straßenverkehrsgesetz) imposes **strict liability** on the registered owner of a motor vehicle for all accidents involving vulnerable road users; fault by the VRU only affects the apportionment of damages, not the existence of liability.
- **Belgium**: since 1994, drivers are presumed liable in collisions with cyclists and pedestrians unless they can demonstrate the VRU acted wilfully or with gross negligence.
- **France**: the **Loi Badinter (1985)** provides that victims of road traffic accidents involving motor vehicles are entitled to full compensation regardless of their own fault, except where they voluntarily caused the accident — a rule widely praised as the strongest VRU protection in the EU.

The European Commission's 2023 revision of the **Motor Insurance Directive (MID)** required all member states to ensure that motor insurance policies cover VRU injuries at a minimum of **€6.07 million per incident** for personal injury and **€1.22 million for property damage**, ensuring that financial barriers do not impede VRU compensation claims.

Vision Zero targets for 2030 require a 50 % reduction from the 2019 baseline of approximately **22,700 road deaths** in the EU-27. Achieving the target would mean no more than **11,350 deaths per year** by 2030, with VRU protection measures estimated to contribute roughly 30 % of the required reduction according to the European Transport Safety Council (ETSC).
