# Low-Emission and Environmental Zones

## Overview and Policy Context

Low-Emission Zones (LEZs) are geographically defined urban areas where access for motor vehicles is restricted or prohibited based on the vehicle's exhaust emissions standard. The policy goal is to reduce concentrations of nitrogen dioxide (NO2), particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), and other pollutants that exceed EU air quality thresholds set by Directive 2008/50/EC. As of 2024, more than 320 LEZs operate across EU member states, with the heaviest concentration in Germany, France, the Netherlands, Italy, and the United Kingdom (prior to Brexit). Some cities operate a stricter variant called an Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ), which imposes tighter Euro-standard thresholds and sometimes applies 24 hours a day, seven days a week. Access control is enforced by a combination of physical inspection, roadside camera networks with automatic number plate recognition (ANPR), and, in Germany specifically, by a coloured windscreen sticker called the Umweltplakette.

## The German Umweltplakette System

Germany introduced the Umweltplakette (environmental badge) scheme under the 35th Federal Immission Control Ordinance (35. BImSchV) in 2008. Every petrol or diesel vehicle driven in a German LEZ — known as an Umweltzone — must display a valid badge on the inside of the windscreen. The badge is a 10 cm circular disc printed on reflective foil and is issued by authorised service providers including vehicle inspection bodies (TUV, DEKRA), vehicle registration offices (Kfz-Zulassungsstellen), and many petrol stations and workshops.

There are three badge colours:

- **Red (Schadstoffgruppe 2):** Assigned to vehicles meeting Euro 1 or the earliest diesel emission standards. Red badges were valid in the least restrictive zones, but by 2012 virtually all German Umweltzonen had prohibited red-badge vehicles.
- **Yellow (Schadstoffgruppe 3):** Assigned to vehicles meeting Euro 2 for petrol engines or Euro 2/3 for older diesels (typically pre-2001 petrol and pre-2005 diesel vehicles that do not qualify for green). Yellow badges are no longer accepted in the majority of German LEZs.
- **Green (Schadstoffgruppe 4):** Assigned to vehicles meeting Euro 3 or higher for petrol and Euro 4 or higher for diesel (or any Euro 3+ diesel fitted with a certified particulate filter). The green badge is the only badge accepted in all currently active German Umweltzonen.

A fourth category, **white**, is not a badge colour but a designation for zero-emission vehicles (purely electric, hydrogen fuel-cell) that are exempt from the sticker requirement entirely. The badge costs approximately €5–€10 from official issuers. Foreign vehicles must obtain and display the correct badge before entering an Umweltzone; the nationality of the vehicle does not confer any exemption.

## German Umweltzone: Key Cities and Penalties

Berlin operates one of Germany's largest Umweltzonen, covering most of the area within the S-Bahn ring road. Only green-badge (Schadstoffgruppe 4) vehicles are permitted. Munich, Hamburg, Cologne, Frankfurt, and Stuttgart all maintain active green-only Umweltzonen. Stuttgart is notable for having introduced episodic "Fahrverbote" (driving bans) on days when fine-dust pollution forecasts exceed alert thresholds; on such days, even some green-badge Euro 5 diesel vehicles may be barred from certain roads.

The penalty for driving in a German Umweltzone without a valid Umweltplakette is a fixed fine of **€80** and one point on the driver's licence in the central traffic register (Fahreignungsregister). There is no criminal liability but repeat offences accumulate points. Local police and traffic wardens enforce compliance through random roadside checks and fixed ANPR cameras at zone entrances.

## France's Crit'Air Vignette System

France introduced the Certificat Qualité de l'Air, universally known as the **Crit'Air vignette**, by decree in 2016. The circular sticker is colour-coded and must be affixed to the lower-left corner of the windscreen (viewed from outside the vehicle) when driving in a Zone à Faibles Emissions mobilité (ZFEm) or during a pollution episode alert. The vignette is valid for the lifetime of the vehicle and costs **€3.72** (including postage), ordered exclusively through the official government website (certificat-air.gouv.fr). Counterfeit or unofficial stickers are not legally recognised.

Crit'Air categories run from **0/E** (best) to **5** (worst), with non-certified vehicles (pre-Euro 1, very old vehicles) receiving no sticker and being barred from all ZFEm areas at all times. The category assigned depends on both the fuel type and the Euro emission standard of the vehicle.

| Crit'Air Category | Colour         | Fuel Type          | Euro Standard                     |
|-------------------|----------------|--------------------|-----------------------------------|
| 0 (also called E) | Dark green     | Electric or hydrogen (any)         | N/A — zero tailpipe emissions     |
| 1                 | Violet/purple  | Petrol (gasoline)  | Euro 5 or Euro 6                  |

| 2                 | Yellow         | Petrol             | Euro 4                            |
| 2                 | Yellow         | Diesel             | Euro 5 or Euro 6                  |

Key observation: a modern Euro 6 diesel car receives Crit'Air **2** (yellow), while a Euro 6 petrol car receives Crit'Air **1** (violet). This asymmetry reflects the historically higher NOx and PM emissions from diesel engines even at the same Euro certification level.

## France: Paris ZFEm and Pollution-Alert Restrictions

Paris operates a permanent ZFEm inside the Boulevard Périphérique (the ring road), enforced since 2019. As of July 2023, vehicles with Crit'Air 4 and 5 stickers are banned from the Paris ZFEm at all times (Monday to Friday, 08:00–20:00). Crit'Air 3 vehicles are permitted during normal conditions but are banned during declared pollution-alert episodes (Pic de pollution). Crit'Air 1 and 2 vehicles currently have unrestricted access.

During a formal pollution-alert day — triggered when daily PM10 exceeds 50 µg/m³ for two consecutive days, or NO2 exceeds 200 µg/m³ for one hour — the restriction threshold tightens: Crit'Air 3 vehicles are also excluded. In extreme multi-day alerts, alternating-registration-number restrictions (circulation alternée) may additionally be imposed.

The fine for a passenger car (category M1) found in a Paris ZFEm without the required Crit'Air sticker, or with a sticker below the threshold in force on that day, is **€68** for the first offence. For heavy goods vehicles (category N2/N3) the fine rises to **€450**. Foreign-registered vehicles are subject to the same rules; police are authorised to stop and fine non-compliant foreign drivers at roadside checks.

## Brussels, Belgium: LEZ

Brussels Capital Region introduced its LEZ on 1 January 2018. Unlike Germany and France, Brussels does not use a physical sticker; access compliance is enforced entirely by ANPR cameras reading the licence plate and cross-referencing a central database. Owners of foreign vehicles must register their vehicle at the Brussels LEZ portal (lez.brussels) before entering; failure to pre-register results in a fine equivalent to unregistered access. The minimum Euro standard required for petrol vehicles is **Euro 2** (from 2018), rising over time: Euro 3 from 2020, Euro 4 from 2022. For diesel vehicles the schedule is stricter: Euro 4 required from 2018, Euro 5 from 2020, and Euro 6 from 2025. Violations incur a daily fine of **€350** per day for passenger cars.

## Madrid Central (Zona de Bajas Emisiones Madrid Central)

Madrid's ZBE Madrid Central is a roughly 4.7 km² restricted zone in the city centre, operational since November 2018. Access is controlled by ANPR cameras without any physical badge. The classification system used in Spain is the DGT (Dirección General de Tráfico) environmental label:

- **Zero emissions (CERO):** Electric, hydrogen, plug-in hybrid with ≥40 km zero-emission range — green circular label.
- **ECO:** Non-plug-in hybrid, compressed natural gas, or liquefied petroleum gas vehicles with Euro 5/6 petrol or Euro 6 diesel — blue/green split label.
- **C:** Euro 6 diesel or Euro 4–6 petrol without DGT Zero/ECO qualification — green label with letter C.
- **B:** Euro 5 diesel or Euro 3 petrol — yellow label with letter B.

Vehicles without a DGT label (older Euro 2 and below) cannot enter Madrid Central at any time. Fines for unauthorised entry start at **€90**. Residents of the zone and certified disabled-badge holders are exempt from restrictions.

## Milan Area C and Congestion-Charging Hybrid Models

Milan operates Area C, a congestion-charging zone covering the historic centre (the "Cerchia dei Bastioni", approximately 8.2 km²). Area C is simultaneously a congestion charge and a partial LEZ: the most polluting vehicles (Euro 0, Euro 1 diesel and petrol, Euro 2 diesel) are banned outright regardless of payment, while cleaner vehicles pay a daily charge of **€5.00** for standard passenger cars (€3.00 for residents, capped at 40 days per year). Purely electric vehicles and Euro 6 vehicles with very low emissions are currently **exempt from the charge** but must still carry a valid Milan access permit (accessed via the ATM Milan app or web portal). Area C operates Monday to Friday, 07:30–19:30, excluding public holidays.

The separate Area B covers most of the city of Milan (about 72% of the municipal area) and bans the most polluting diesel vehicles (below Euro 5) on weekdays. Area B is enforced by ANPR cameras at over 180 access points.

## Permanent vs. Episodic Restrictions

European LEZs operate under two distinct restriction modes:

**Permanent restrictions** apply every day (or every weekday) regardless of current air quality. Berlin's Umweltzone, Brussels's LEZ, and most Dutch milieuzone regulations operate in this permanent mode. Once a vehicle class falls below the required threshold, it is barred year-round.

**Episodic (smog-alert) restrictions** are triggered only when real-time or forecast pollution data exceeds defined thresholds. France's "Pic de pollution" system is the primary EU example: Crit'Air 3 vehicles that are otherwise tolerated in the Paris ZFEm are banned on alert days. Germany's Stuttgart Feinstaubalarm (fine-dust alarm) similarly restricts certain Euro 5 diesel vehicles on high-pollution days, typically in autumn and winter when temperature inversions trap pollutants.

Some cities apply a layered approach: permanent baseline restrictions for the oldest vehicles combined with episodic additional restrictions for intermediate categories. This tiered model is increasingly favoured because it limits economic disruption while still providing a regulatory backstop during health-risk events.

## Exemptions Across Systems

Despite variation between countries, several exemption categories are common across EU LEZ schemes:

**Zero-emission vehicles (ZEVs):** Battery electric vehicles (BEVs) and hydrogen fuel-cell vehicles (FCEVs) are exempt from badge or sticker requirements and access fees in virtually all EU LEZ systems, including the German Umweltzonen, French ZFEm, Brussels LEZ, and Milan Area C. In Germany this exemption is statutory under the 35. BImSchV.

**Disabled badge holders:** Vehicles displaying a valid EU-format disabled parking badge (the blue badge with the International Symbol of Access) receive exemptions in most city LEZs. In Paris, blue-badge holders are exempt from ZFEm access restrictions. In Milan, "disabili" permit holders are exempt from Area C charges.

**Emergency and essential services:** Ambulances, fire engines, police, and military vehicles are universally exempt. Some cities also exempt agricultural machinery, hearses, and historic vehicles (typically over 30 years old).

**Temporary exemptions and derogations:** Short-term visitor permits can sometimes be obtained from city authorities (e.g., for a tradesperson making a one-off delivery); these vary by city and are not standardised across the EU.

**Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs):** Treatment varies. Spain's DGT grants ECO-label status to PHEVs meeting specific zero-emission range criteria. In Paris, PHEVs that meet Euro 6 petrol standard generally receive Crit'Air 1. In Germany, PHEVs must still carry an Umweltplakette based on their combustion engine's Euro rating.

## Sticker Procurement and Cross-Border Use

A practical challenge for cross-border travellers is that sticker systems are national and not mutually recognised. A French Crit'Air vignette does not satisfy the German Umweltplakette requirement, and vice versa. A driver travelling from the Netherlands through Germany into France and onward to Spain must potentially carry or register for four separate systems (Dutch milieuzone — camera-only, German Umweltplakette physical sticker, French Crit'Air physical sticker, Spanish DGT label).

The European Commission has proposed harmonisation under the Euro VI/VII framework discussions, and a 2023 European Parliament resolution called for a single EU-wide green vehicle label, but as of 2024 no binding harmonisation directive has been adopted. In the interim, national transport authorities maintain information portals, and navigation apps such as Google Maps and TomTom have begun integrating LEZ boundary warnings and sticker-requirement notifications for routes crossing multiple countries.
