# EU Driving Licences

## Legal Basis and the Harmonised Licence Format

The EU driving licence is governed by Directive 2006/126/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council, adopted on 20 December 2006 and fully applicable from 19 January 2013. It replaced the previous Directive 91/439/EEC and introduced a single, standardised credit-card photocard format across all member states. The physical document measures 85.6 mm × 54 mm (ISO/IEC 7810 ID-1), carries the holder's photo, date of birth, issuing authority, and a machine-readable zone. The front of the card shows the EU flag symbol and the word "DRIVING LICENCE" in the official language(s) of the issuing state. Each category held is listed on the back with its issue date, expiry date, and any restriction codes (harmonised codes 01–99). Member states may add a microchip but this is optional. The licence is issued by the member state of normal residence; "normal residence" is defined as living in that state for at least 185 days per calendar year for personal reasons, or for occupational reasons if also studying there.

## Licence Categories — Two-Wheeled and Light Vehicles

| Category | Vehicle Type | Key Limits | Minimum Age |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| AM | Mopeds and light quadricycles | Max speed 45 km/h; engine ≤ 50 cc (spark-ignition) or max net power ≤ 4 kW (other engines); quadricycles ≤ 350 kg unladen | 16 |
| A1 | Light motorcycles | Engine capacity ≤ 125 cc; power ≤ 11 kW; power-to-weight ratio ≤ 0.1 kW/kg | 16 |
| A2 | Medium motorcycles | Power ≤ 35 kW; power-to-weight ratio ≤ 0.2 kW/kg; not derived from a vehicle of more than double its power | 18 |
| A | All motorcycles, including motor tricycles | Unrestricted; motor tricycles > 15 kW require minimum age 21 | 20 (progressive) or 24 (direct access) |
| B1 | Light quadricycles (motorised) | Unladen mass ≤ 400 kg (550 kg for goods vehicles); power ≤ 15 kW | 16 |

Category AM is the entry-level category. An A1 licence also entitles the holder to drive AM-category vehicles. An A2 holder may drive A1 vehicles. A full A licence covers A2 and A1 vehicles as well.

Progressive access to category A requires holding A2 for at least two years. Direct access at age 24 bypasses the A2 stage entirely.

## Category B — The Standard Car Licence

Category B covers motor vehicles with a maximum authorised mass (MAM) not exceeding 3,500 kg and designed to carry no more than 8 passengers in addition to the driver (i.e., up to 9 occupants total). A trailer may be towed if the MAM of the trailer does not exceed 750 kg. If the trailer MAM exceeds 750 kg, the combined MAM of the combination must not exceed 3,500 kg and the trailer MAM must not exceed the unladen mass of the towing vehicle.

The minimum age for category B is **18 years**. However, member states may lower this to **17 years** for accompanied driving, where a supervising driver (typically age 25 or older, holding a B licence for at least three years) accompanies the novice. This scheme is called Accompanied Driving (AD) or, in some national implementations, "B17."

Category B also entitles the holder to drive AM-category mopeds without any further test in most member states.

Category B1 is optional for member states; not all EU countries issue it.

## Category BE and Trailer Combinations

Category BE allows a category B licence holder to tow heavier trailers. Specifically, BE covers combinations where the towing vehicle is in category B and the trailer or semi-trailer has a MAM exceeding 750 kg, provided the combination itself exceeds 3,500 kg MAM. The minimum age for BE is **18 years**, matching category B.

There is no separate "E" category in isolation; the "E" suffix always follows a base category (BE, C1E, CE, D1E, DE). These so-called "combination" categories permit the holder to drive the corresponding tractor unit with a heavier trailer. Holding CE automatically grants C1E entitlement; holding DE grants D1E entitlement.

## Categories C and C1 — Lorries and Goods Vehicles

| Category | Vehicle Type | Key Limits | Minimum Age |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| C1 | Medium lorries | MAM > 3,500 kg but ≤ 7,500 kg; may tow trailer up to 750 kg | 18 |
| C | Large lorries (HGV) | MAM > 3,500 kg (no upper limit); may tow trailer up to 750 kg | 21 |
| C1E | C1 vehicle + heavy trailer | Combined MAM ≤ 12,000 kg; trailer MAM may exceed 750 kg | 18 |
| CE | C vehicle + heavy trailer | No MAM ceiling on combination | 21 |

Holding a full C licence automatically grants C1 entitlement. C1 does not grant C entitlement. A C licence also requires the holder to have, or simultaneously obtain, a category B licence.

Professional drivers of C/CE vehicles engaged in commercial carriage of goods must additionally hold a **Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (CPC)** under Directive 2003/59/EC, requiring 35 hours of periodic training every five years.

## Categories D and D1 — Buses and Passenger Vehicles

| Category | Vehicle Type | Key Limits | Minimum Age |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| D1 | Minibuses | More than 8 passenger seats but not more than 16 passenger seats; length ≤ 8 m | 21 |
| D | Full-size buses and coaches | More than 8 passenger seats; no upper limit on seats or length | 24 |
| D1E | D1 vehicle + heavy trailer | Trailer MAM > 750 kg; combined MAM ≤ 12,000 kg | 21 |
| DE | D vehicle + heavy trailer | Trailer MAM > 750 kg | 24 |

Category D requires the applicant to already hold, or simultaneously pass, category B. Holding D grants D1 entitlement. Drivers of D/DE vehicles in commercial passenger transport must also hold a **Driver CPC** with the same 35-hour/5-year periodic training requirement as C category drivers.

The minimum age for category D may be lowered to **21 years** by member states for drivers operating on national territory only, provided those drivers hold a Driver CPC.

## Administrative Validity Periods

Directive 2006/126/EC mandates maximum administrative validity periods for newly issued licences. The physical card must be renewed at these intervals even if the driving entitlement itself is not reassessed; renewal provides an opportunity to update the photo and verify continued residence.

| Licence Group | Maximum Administrative Validity |
|---------------|--------------------------------|
| AM, A1, A2, A (motorcycles/mopeds) | 10 years |
| B, B1, BE (cars and light combinations) | 10 years |
| C1, C, C1E, CE (lorries) | 5 years |
| D1, D, D1E, DE (buses) | 5 years |

Member states may issue B-category licences with an administrative validity of up to **15 years** for cards issued before the holder turns 65. After the age of 65, member states may impose shorter renewal intervals. The shorter 5-year validity for C and D categories is directly linked to the mandatory medical fitness checks required at each renewal for those groups.

## Medical Requirements

For category B holders, the initial licence application requires a self-declaration of medical fitness. No mandatory periodic medical examination is imposed at the EU level for B-category renewals, although member states may require one for older drivers (commonly from age 70).

For categories C, C1, D, and D1 (and their E variants), a medical examination by an approved medical practitioner is **mandatory** at each licence renewal. The examination covers:

- **Visual acuity:** binocular visual acuity of at least 0.8 (right and left eye separately at least 0.1); horizontal visual field of at least 160°.
- **Cardiovascular fitness:** absence of conditions likely to cause sudden incapacitation.
- **Hearing:** sufficient hearing, possibly with a hearing aid.
- **Neurological and psychiatric assessment** as clinically indicated.

Diabetes mellitus managed with insulin is not an automatic bar to C/D categories but requires specialist assessment and, typically, annual medical review.

## Mutual Recognition and Licence Exchange

A driving licence issued by any EU member state is automatically recognised in all other member states. A holder may use their licence throughout the EU without conversion as long as it remains valid and the holder satisfies any age requirements of the host state.

When a holder **takes up normal residence** in a new member state, they are not obliged to exchange their licence immediately, but they may choose to do so. The host state must accept the licence and may transcribe the details into its national records. The host state may apply its own validity period (e.g., 10 years for category B) to the newly issued card but must not alter the driving entitlements, restrict categories without cause, or require the holder to retake a driving test.

If the licence is due for renewal because it has reached the end of its administrative validity, the holder must renew with the member state of current normal residence, not the issuing state.

Third-country licences (e.g., a UK licence after Brexit, or a US licence) are not subject to automatic recognition. Exchange rules for these are set bilaterally by each member state.

## Novice Driver Provisions and Probationary Periods

Directive 2006/126/EC leaves detailed probationary/novice-driver regimes to member states but sets a framework. New B-category holders are considered novice drivers and may be subject to national restrictions during an initial period, typically two years. Common national measures include:

- **Lower blood alcohol limit** (many states impose 0.0 g/100 ml or 0.2 g/100 ml for novices vs. the general 0.5 g/100 ml).
- **Night driving restrictions** or passenger limits for young drivers (national implementation varies).
- **Points-based systems** where novices start with fewer points, or where accumulation of penalty points leads to mandatory retraining before the licence becomes permanent.

The **Accompanied Driving (AD)** scheme (minimum age 17 in member states that adopt it) is specifically mentioned in the Directive as a mechanism to extend supervised on-road experience before solo driving. Supervisors must hold the relevant category and must have held it for at least **three years**.

Some states operate a **two-phase system**: the first phase is a probationary licence, and the second (full) licence is issued only after passing an additional hazard-perception or second-phase examination.

## Replacing, Renewing, and Duplicate Licences

Renewal is the process of obtaining a new card at the end of an administrative validity period; entitlements are not reassessed unless a medical issue arises. The application must be made to the competent authority of the **member state of normal residence**.

Replacement is required when a licence is lost, stolen, or damaged. The replacing state is the state of normal residence (or, if the original licence was issued there and the holder still lives there, the same state). A replacement does not reset the administrative validity clock; the replacement card carries the same expiry date as the lost card would have had (unless renewal is also due).

Downgrading (removal of a category) can only occur on medical grounds or as a sanction under national law. Member states must notify the issuing state when they downgrade or revoke a licence originally issued by another member state.

Fees and processing times for renewals are set nationally. The Directive does not harmonise fees.
