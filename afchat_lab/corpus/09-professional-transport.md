# Professional and Commercial Road Transport

## Overview and Regulatory Framework

Professional road transport in the European Union is governed by a dense web of directly applicable regulations designed to ensure driver safety, fair competition, and road safety across all 27 member states. The cornerstone legislation is **Regulation (EC) No 561/2006**, which establishes harmonised rules on driving times, breaks, and rest periods for drivers of heavy goods vehicles (HGVs) and passenger-carrying vehicles (PCVs). This regulation replaced the earlier Regulation (EEC) No 3820/85 and has been amended by Regulation (EU) 2020/1054, which introduced new provisions on the return of drivers to their home base and the prohibition on taking regular weekly rest in the cab.

The regulation applies to carriage by road in vehicles with a maximum permissible mass (MPM) exceeding 3.5 tonnes, including the trailer or semi-trailer, and to vehicles constructed or adapted to carry more than nine passengers including the driver. Exemptions exist for certain national operations — for instance, vehicles used for the carriage of passengers on regular services where the route does not exceed 50 km, vehicles with a maximum authorised speed not exceeding 40 km/h, and certain agricultural, forestry, or construction vehicles operating within a 100 km radius of their base.

## Driving Time Limits Under Regulation (EC) 561/2006

The regulation imposes strict limits on how long a driver may be at the wheel before taking a break, as well as cumulative daily and weekly ceilings.

**Continuous driving limit:** A driver must not drive for more than **4 hours 30 minutes** without taking an uninterrupted break of at least 45 minutes. This break may alternatively be replaced by a break of at least 15 minutes followed by a break of at least 30 minutes distributed over the period, but never taken in reverse order. The 45-minute break must be completed before the driving time limit is reached, not after.

**Daily driving limit:** The total daily driving time must not exceed **9 hours**. This may be extended to **10 hours** not more than **twice during any one calendar week**. A "day" in this context is the period between any two consecutive daily rest periods, or between a daily rest period and a weekly rest period.

**Weekly driving limit:** The total accumulated driving time during any single week must not exceed **56 hours**.

**Fortnightly driving limit:** The total accumulated driving time in any **two consecutive weeks** must not exceed **90 hours**.

| Rule | Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Continuous driving before break | 4 h 30 min | Break of 45 min required (or 15 + 30 min split) |
| Daily driving (standard) | 9 hours | Extendable in certain conditions |
| Daily driving (extended) | 10 hours | Maximum twice per calendar week |
| Weekly driving limit | 56 hours | Single week |
| Fortnightly driving limit | 90 hours | Any two consecutive weeks |
| Daily rest (regular) | 11 hours | Consecutive hours |
| Daily rest (reduced) | 9 hours | Not more than 3 times between weekly rests |
| Weekly rest (regular) | 45 hours | Must begin by end of sixth 24-hour period |
| Weekly rest (reduced) | 24 hours | Compensation required within 3 weeks |

## Mandatory Breaks and Daily Rest Periods

**Breaks:** After driving for 4 hours and 30 minutes, the driver must take a break of at least 45 minutes. If a split break is used, the first portion must be at least 15 minutes and the second at least 30 minutes, taken in that order. During a break, the driver may not carry out any other work, but may use the time for waiting, passive attendance, or personal activities.

**Daily rest:** A driver must take a daily rest period of at least **11 consecutive hours** in every 24-hour period following a weekly rest period. This may be reduced to a minimum of **9 consecutive hours** (a "reduced daily rest") no more than **three times** between any two consecutive weekly rest periods. No compensation is required for a reduced daily rest. A regular daily rest may alternatively be taken in two separate periods: the first must be at least 3 uninterrupted hours and the second at least 9 uninterrupted hours, making a total of at least 12 hours; this counts as a regular (not reduced) daily rest.

**Multi-manning:** Where two drivers are on board (multi-manning), each must take a daily rest of at least 9 hours within each 30-hour period measured from the end of the last daily or weekly rest period.

## Weekly Rest Rules and the 2020 Amendment

Under Article 8 of Regulation (EC) 561/2006, a driver must take a **regular weekly rest of at least 45 consecutive hours** at the end of, at most, six consecutive 24-hour periods following the previous weekly rest period.

A **reduced weekly rest** of not less than **24 consecutive hours** may be taken instead, subject to compensation. The reduction must be compensated, en bloc, by an equivalent rest attached to a rest period of at least 9 hours before the end of the **third week** following the week in question.

Regulation (EU) 2020/1054 added a critical prohibition: drivers may **not take a regular weekly rest period in the cab of their vehicle**. The vehicle's sleeping berth is insufficient for a regular weekly rest. Operators must organise work in such a way that the driver can return to the operator's base or to their place of residence for the purpose of taking regular weekly rest. The operator must document how this obligation is met and retain this documentation for inspection.

For international transport, drivers operating outside their member state of establishment may take up to **two consecutive reduced weekly rests** (minimum 24 hours each) before being obliged to take a regular weekly rest. After two consecutive reduced weekly rests, the driver must take a regular weekly rest outside the vehicle.

## The Digital Tachograph

Regulation (EU) No 165/2014 governs tachograph requirements. Every vehicle subject to Regulation (EC) 561/2006 must be fitted with a tachograph — a tamper-evident recording device that automatically records driving speed, distance, and activity.

**Smart tachographs (version 2, "smart 2"):** Since **21 August 2023**, all new vehicles registered and subject to tachograph requirements must be fitted with a second-generation smart tachograph. Smart tachographs include GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) positioning, ITS roadside communication capability, and remote early detection capability allowing roadside enforcement officers to read data without stopping the vehicle.

Vehicles in international transport that were previously fitted with first-generation smart tachographs (or analogue tachographs) had to be retrofitted with second-generation smart tachographs by **31 December 2024** for those originally fitted with analogue or older digital tachographs used in international haulage.

**Driver card:** Each driver must carry a personal **driver card**, issued by the competent authority of their member state of residence. The card stores up to **28 days** of activity data. Cards are valid for **5 years** and are personal — a driver may not hold more than one valid driver card at a time.

**Tachograph records:** Operators must retain tachograph records (data downloaded from the vehicle unit and from driver cards) for a minimum of **12 months**. Downloading must occur at least every **90 days** from the vehicle unit and every **28 days** from the driver card. During roadside inspections, drivers must produce records for the current day and the previous **28 calendar days**, plus the driver's current driver card.

## HGV Definitions, Weights, and Dimensions

In EU law, a **heavy goods vehicle (HGV)** is generally defined as a motor vehicle used for the carriage of goods with a maximum authorised mass (MAM) exceeding **3.5 tonnes**. The term "lorry" is the common British English equivalent; in EU legislative texts the terms "goods vehicle" or "motor vehicle" are typically used with reference to MAM thresholds.

**Maximum weights:**

- Standard maximum gross vehicle weight (GVW) for a rigid HGV on public roads: **18 tonnes** (two axles) or up to **32 tonnes** (three or more axles, depending on axle spacing).
- Maximum gross train weight for an articulated vehicle or road train: **40 tonnes** under Directive 96/53/EC (as amended).
- **Intermodal transport exception:** Where a semi-trailer carries one or more ISO containers or swap bodies, the maximum is raised to **44 tonnes** (for a five- or six-axle combination) to encourage modal shift.
- Maximum weight per axle: **11.5 tonnes** for a single drive axle; **21 tonnes** for a tandem axle grouping (with some member states granting higher limits for road-friendly suspensions).

**Maximum dimensions under Directive 96/53/EC:**

- Maximum **length** of a motor vehicle: **12 metres** (rigid); **16.5 metres** (articulated lorry/semi-trailer combination); **18.75 metres** (road train / drawbar combination).
- Maximum **width**: **2.55 metres** for most goods vehicles; **2.60 metres** for insulated/refrigerated vehicles.
- Maximum **height**: **4.0 metres** (no EU-wide mandatory limit, but 4.0 m is the standard nationally set limit in most member states; some, such as Sweden and Finland, allow 4.2–4.5 m under special provisions).

EU Directive 2015/719 introduced provisions for **longer and heavier vehicles (LHVs)** on a bilateral or national basis, allowing combinations up to **25.25 metres** when not crossing borders (unless both member states agree).

## Certificate of Professional Competence (CPC)

Directive 2003/59/EC, implemented by all EU member states, requires all professional drivers of HGVs (Category C, C1, CE, C1E) and passenger-carrying vehicles (Category D, D1, DE, D1E) to hold a **Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (Driver CPC)**. The certificate is distinct from the driving licence itself.

**Initial CPC:** To obtain the initial CPC, a driver must pass both a **theory test** (two parts: multiple choice and case-study questions) and a **practical driving test** (two parts). The initial qualification process is designed to ensure that drivers demonstrate knowledge of road transport legislation, technical and mechanical aspects, emergency procedures, health and safety, customer service, and commercial and financial management of transport operations.

**Periodic training:** Once the initial CPC is obtained, drivers must complete **35 hours of periodic training** every **5 years** to maintain their CPC. The 35 hours must be completed in courses approved by the relevant national authority, in modules of at least **7 hours** per session (which may be spread over two days). Training can include a maximum of **12 hours** on an approved computer-based or e-learning platform.

**DQC card:** The CPC qualification is evidenced by a **Driver Qualification Card (DQC)**, issued by the competent authority. The DQC is separate from the driving licence but is carried alongside it. Failure to hold a valid DQC when required constitutes a criminal offence in most member states.

Drivers who obtained their Category C or D licence **before the directive's deadlines** (10 September 2009 for HGV drivers; 10 September 2008 for bus/coach drivers) were granted **acquired rights** — they were deemed to hold the initial CPC without further testing, but still must complete periodic training.

## ADR: Transport of Dangerous Goods by Road

The **European Agreement concerning the International Carriage of Dangerous Goods by Road (ADR)** is a UN-ECE treaty that governs the transportation of hazardous materials. In EU law it is given effect by **Directive 2008/68/EC** (as amended), which extends ADR requirements to domestic as well as international journeys within the EU.

**Classification:** ADR divides dangerous goods into **9 classes** (e.g., Class 1 — Explosives; Class 2 — Gases; Class 3 — Flammable liquids; Class 6.1 — Toxic substances; Class 8 — Corrosive substances; Class 9 — Miscellaneous dangerous substances and articles). Each class contains one or more divisions reflecting different hazard profiles.

**Driver training (ADR certificate):** Drivers carrying dangerous goods in quantities above the exemption thresholds in Chapter 1.1.3 of ADR must hold an **ADR certificate** (also called an ADR vocational training certificate). The training consists of a **core module** and, where relevant, specialisation modules for tanks, explosives, or radioactive materials. Certificates are valid for **5 years** and renewal requires passing a re-examination or top-up course.

**Vehicle requirements:** Vehicles carrying dangerous goods must display **orange rectangular plates** (unlit, reflective): a plain orange plate at front and rear for mixed loads, or plates bearing the **hazard identification number (HIN/Kemler code)** on top and the **UN number** on the bottom for single-substance tank loads. Vehicles must also display the appropriate **hazard warning diamonds (danger labels)** on the sides and rear of the load compartment.

**Safety adviser:** Undertakings involved in the transport, loading, or unloading of dangerous goods must appoint a **Dangerous Goods Safety Adviser (DGSA)** (unless benefiting from an exemption under 1.8.3.2 ADR). The DGSA must hold a certificate obtained by passing a written examination administered by the competent authority. The certificate is valid for **5 years**.

**Exemptions:** The quantities-based exemption in ADR (1.1.3.6) allows transport of certain small quantities without full ADR compliance, provided the total transport index or aggregate quantity does not exceed specified thresholds per dangerous-goods transport unit.

## Enforcement, Penalties, and Record-Keeping

Enforcement of driving time rules and tachograph requirements is carried out by national enforcement authorities — typically the traffic police and specialist transport inspectors — with harmonisation under **Directive 2006/22/EC**. Member states must conduct a minimum proportion of their roadside checks using tachograph data analysis.

**ERRU (European Register of Road Transport Undertakings):** All transport operators holding a Community licence must be registered in their national electronic register, which is interconnected via ERRU across all member states. Serious infringements recorded against an operator or driver in one state are visible to authorities in all others.

**Categorisation of infringements:** Under Regulation (EU) 2016/403, infringements of drivers' hours and tachograph rules are classified as **most serious infringements (MSI)**, **very serious infringements (VSI)**, or **serious infringements (SI)** depending on the magnitude of the breach. Repeated MSIs can trigger proceedings to revoke the operator's Community licence and the transport manager's good repute.

**Immobilisation:** Roadside enforcement officers may immobilise a vehicle if a driver has violated daily driving limits by more than 50%, has not taken a required break, or is found to have manipulated or tampered with the tachograph. An immobilisation order may not be lifted until sufficient rest has been taken.

**Records retention:** Operators must retain tachograph records, driver cards data, manual entries, and exception records for a minimum of **12 months**. In some member states the requirement is 24 months for operator inspection purposes.

## Cabotage and International Haulage Rules

**Community licence:** An HGV operator wishing to carry goods for hire or reward internationally within the EU must hold a **Community licence** (a certified copy travels with each vehicle) issued under Regulation (EC) No 1072/2009.

**Cabotage:** Cabotage — the carriage of goods between two points in the same member state by a vehicle registered in a different member state — is permitted under strict conditions. Under Regulation (EU) 2020/1055 (the "Mobility Package"), after completing an international journey into a host member state, a haulier may perform a maximum of **3 cabotage operations** within **7 days** of the date of the final delivery in the international haul. After the 7-day period, the vehicle must leave the host member state and observe a **cooling-off period of 4 days** before returning to perform further cabotage.

**Posting of drivers:** Regulation (EU) 2020/1057 established lex specialis rules for the posting of drivers in road transport. Drivers performing bilateral transport operations or transit are excluded from full posting obligations; drivers performing cabotage operations are subject to host member state terms and conditions of employment from the first day, including minimum wage requirements.
