# Driving Abroad in the EU

## Overview

Driving across European borders is straightforward for residents of EU member states and closely associated countries, but it requires preparation. Drivers must carry a specific set of documents, equip their vehicles for host-country requirements, and understand the toll and vignette systems in each country they plan to transit. Failure to comply can result in on-the-spot fines that in some countries may be collected immediately by roadside police. This document covers the rules applicable to EU-wide cross-border driving, with particular focus on documentation, mandatory equipment, vignettes, toll systems, and emergency procedures.

---

## Documents Required When Driving Abroad

Every driver operating a motor vehicle in another EU country must carry and be able to produce on demand the following documents:

- **Driving licence** — A valid national driving licence issued by an EU/EEA state is recognised throughout the EU without an International Driving Permit (IDP). UK licence-holders driving in EU member states after Brexit still have full recognition under the 1968 Vienna Convention, but an IDP (1968 Vienna Convention type) is additionally recommended for countries that are parties only to the 1949 Geneva Convention. The licence must not be expired.
- **Vehicle registration document** — The original registration certificate (in the UK: the V5C logbook, or a VE103 letter from the vehicle's registered keeper if the car is hired or leased). A photocopy is not accepted as a substitute for the original in most EU countries.
- **Insurance certificate / proof of valid motor insurance** — A current certificate of motor insurance showing at minimum third-party liability cover, which is the EU-mandated minimum. Insurers in EU member states are obliged to provide at least third-party liability coverage that extends automatically to all other EU member states.
- **Identity document** — A valid national identity card (for EU/EEA citizens) or a valid passport. Drivers should carry whichever their country of origin issues; some EU countries require a passport rather than an ID card for non-EU visitors.

## The Green Card (International Motor Insurance Certificate)

The Green Card is the internationally recognised proof of motor insurance, formally called the International Motor Insurance Certificate (IMIC). It is issued by the driver's insurer, typically on green paper (though the requirement for green paper was removed in 2020 and it may now be printed on white paper), and it follows the standard format established by the Council of Bureaux.

Within the EU and EEA, the Green Card is not strictly required because EU Directive 2009/103/EC mandates that all registered vehicles carry at least third-party liability cover valid across all member states. However, the Green Card remains the simplest way to prove the territorial extent of cover at a border crossing or after a road accident. It is still required when entering certain non-EU countries that are members of the Green Card system — including Serbia, Bosnia-Herzegovina, Turkey, Morocco, and Tunisia — and is strongly recommended when driving in any country outside the EU.

If a driver is involved in an accident abroad and the at-fault driver cannot be identified or is uninsured, the host country's national motor insurer bureau (a member of the Green Card system) handles the claim on behalf of the victim.

## Country Identification: Oval Stickers and Number-Plate Codes

Historically, all vehicles driven outside their home country were required to display a white oval nationality sticker on the rear of the vehicle showing the country code (e.g., **D** for Germany, **F** for France, **I** for Italy, **E** for Spain, **NL** for Netherlands, **A** for Austria, **CH** for Switzerland, **CZ** for Czechia, **PL** for Poland). Within the EU, this requirement was relaxed: if the number plate already incorporates the EU blue band and the country code letters, a separate oval sticker is not required.

A notable recent change concerns the United Kingdom. After Brexit, the UK changed its official country code from **GB** (Great Britain — the code historically displayed on British vehicles since the 1949 Geneva Convention) to **UK** effective 28 September 2021. Vehicles with number plates displaying the old **GB** identifier on the EU-style blue band were required to update to **UK**, or to add a separate **UK** oval sticker. The old GB sticker is no longer valid for international use; using it may result in a fine when crossing into countries that enforce the updated code.

Non-EU countries — most notably Switzerland — still require a separate oval country-code sticker unless the number plate already carries a conforming national identifier.

## Driving on the Right and Headlamp Beam Deflectors

All EU member states require driving on the **right-hand side** of the road, except **Malta** and **Cyprus**, which — like the United Kingdom and Ireland — drive on the **left**. Drivers from right-hand-drive countries (UK, Ireland, Malta, Cyprus) who take their vehicles to continental EU countries face a specific hazard: the headlamps of right-hand-drive cars are aligned to dip to the left, which on right-hand-drive roads keeps the beam low on the near side. On the continent, where oncoming traffic comes from the other direction, the same beam pattern dips toward oncoming drivers, causing dazzle.

To correct this, drivers of right-hand-drive cars must fit **headlamp beam deflectors** (also called headlamp adapters or beam converters) before driving on the continent. These are self-adhesive opaque film strips that attach to the lens of halogen headlamps to mask the upward-angled portion of the beam. They are widely available at UK motor accessory retailers for approximately £5–£15 per set. Deflectors designed for one headlamp type (e.g., H4 bulbs with asymmetric reflectors) are not interchangeable with other types; the correct deflector depends on headlamp design. Vehicles with automatic self-levelling or adaptive headlamps usually have a beam-pattern adjustment accessible through the dashboard menu or a small switch rather than using adhesive deflectors.

Failure to fit beam deflectors when driving a right-hand-drive vehicle in France, Belgium, Germany, or any other right-hand-traffic country is a traffic offence and may result in a fine.

## Mandatory Equipment by Country

EU member states have diverging requirements for equipment that must be carried in the vehicle. The table below is illustrative; always verify current requirements before travelling.

| Equipment item | France | Germany | Spain | Italy | Austria |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reflective warning triangle (×1) | Required | Required | Required (×2) | Required | Required |
| High-visibility vest (×1 per occupant) | Required in car | Required in car | Required in car | Required in car | Required in car |
| First-aid kit | Not required | Required | Not required | Not required | Required |
| Fire extinguisher | Not required | Not required | Not required | Required (optional in practice) | Not required |
| Spare bulb kit | Recommended | Not required | Recommended | Not required | Not required |
| Breathalyser (NF-approved) | Was required; now only recommended | Not required | Not required | Not required | Not required |

**Key rules to note:**
- **France** formerly required each occupant to carry a certified NF-approved single-use breathalyser. Since 2020, the mandatory fine for not carrying one was removed, but it is still recommended.
- **Spain** requires two triangles (one to be placed at the front of the vehicle, one at the rear).
- **Germany** requires a first-aid kit that must be within its use-by date.
- **Austria** requires both a warning triangle and a first-aid kit, plus high-visibility vests for all occupants.
- High-visibility vests must generally be stored inside the passenger compartment (not in the boot/trunk), so they can be put on before exiting the car on a motorway.

## Motorway Tolls and Electronic Toll Tags

Several EU countries operate **distance-based motorway toll systems**, where payment is made at toll booths or electronically as you travel a specific section.

- **France** has an extensive tolled autoroute network operated by concession companies (Vinci, Sanef, APRR, etc.). Payment is accepted in cash, by card at attended or automatic lanes, or via the **Télépéage** electronic tag (Liber-t tag). Non-French residents can obtain a compatible tag; alternatively, a **Eurotoll** or **Bip&Go** account provides a tag valid on French and some Spanish motorways.
- **Italy** operates a similar distance-based system (Autostrade per l'Italia). The Italian electronic tag system is **Telepass**, which also works in France, Spain, Portugal, and on the Mont Blanc and Fréjus tunnels. Foreign drivers without a tag use cash or card lanes.
- **Spain** has tolled autopistas (designation AP-) on many major routes. The **Via-T** system is the domestic electronic tag, but Telepass is also accepted on many routes.
- **Portugal** has largely cashless toll motorways; foreign drivers without a Portuguese **Via Verde** tag must register at the border or rent a temporary device, or risk toll violation notices.

The **European Electronic Toll Service (EETS)** is an EU initiative (Directive 2019/520/EU) designed to allow a single on-board unit to pay tolls across all EU member states. Full interoperability is still being phased in as of 2024–2025.

## Vignette Systems

Several European countries — particularly in central Europe — operate **time-based vignette systems** rather than distance-based tolls. A vignette (also called a motorway sticker or road tax sticker) gives unlimited access to the motorway network for a set duration. Failure to display or register a valid vignette can result in on-the-spot fines of €50–€500 depending on country.

**Austria detail:** The Austrian motorway vignette (Pickerl) is available in 10-day (~€9.90), 2-month (~€29.00), and annual (~€96.40) versions. Since January 2022, Austria has moved to a fully digital system for the 1-day, 10-day, and 2-month options; physical stickers are still available for the annual version. Buyers register their number plate at an official sales point (ASFINAG partner), at border petrol stations, or via the ASFINAG online portal.

**Switzerland detail:** Switzerland is not an EU member but is included here because it is a common transit country. Its vignette is annual-only, costs CHF 40 (approximately €42 depending on exchange rate), and is valid from 1 December of the year before until 31 January the following year — effectively 14 months. It must be physically affixed to the windscreen (not transferable between vehicles). Digital Switzerland vignettes have been available since 2023 alongside the physical version.

**Czechia detail:** Czechia abolished physical vignettes entirely in February 2021. All vignettes are now registered to the number plate digitally. Options include 1-day (~€2), 10-day (~€7), 30-day (~€12), and annual (~€16.50). Police and ASFINAG/SFDI cameras verify compliance by reading the plate.

## Speed Limits and General Traffic Rules Abroad

Speed limits vary significantly between EU countries and must be observed regardless of the driver's home-country rules.

| Road type | France | Germany | Spain | Italy | Austria |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Urban (built-up area) | 50 km/h (30 km/h in many zones) | 50 km/h | 50 km/h | 50 km/h | 50 km/h |
| Rural (outside built-up) | 80 km/h | 100 km/h | 90 km/h | 90 km/h | 100 km/h |
| Motorway | 130 km/h | No general limit* | 120 km/h | 130 km/h | 130 km/h |

*Germany has no general motorway speed limit on unrestricted Autobahn sections (advisory speed 130 km/h); limits are posted at road works, weather-sensitive sections, and urban approaches.

France reduced its standard rural road speed limit from 90 km/h to **80 km/h** in July 2018 (departments may reinstate 90 km/h on selected roads with central reservations).

Zero-tolerance or near-zero blood alcohol limits apply in several countries. Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary, and Romania enforce a **0.0% BAC (zero tolerance)** limit. Most Western EU countries use **0.5 g/L** (equivalent to 0.5 per mille), while the UK and Malta use **0.8 g/L** — still higher than most EU states.

## Emergency Number 112

The **pan-European emergency number 112** is the single emergency call number that operates in all EU member states and most other European countries, including Turkey, Switzerland, and the UK. It connects callers to police, fire, and ambulance services and can be dialled free of charge from any mobile phone (including phones with no SIM card, or where the home network has no signal but another network is present). 112 is also accessible from landlines.

When calling 112 abroad, the operator may answer in the host country's language; in many countries, English-speaking operators or transfer to an English line is available. In some newer vehicles, **eCall** (introduced as mandatory on new EU passenger cars from April 2018) will automatically dial 112 and transmit the vehicle's GPS location in the event of a serious collision, even if the occupants are unconscious.

Drivers should note that national emergency numbers (e.g., 999 in the UK, 17/15/18 in France, 110/112 in Germany) also continue to work alongside 112 in their respective countries.

## Summary Checklist for Cross-Border EU Driving

Before crossing an EU border, verify the following:

1. **Documents in the vehicle:** driving licence (valid), vehicle registration (original V5C or VE103), insurance certificate / Green Card, passport or national ID card.
2. **Country identification:** correct country-code oval sticker or compliant number plate if the EU blue-band identifier is present; update GB to UK if applicable.
3. **Headlamp beam deflectors:** fitted if driving a right-hand-drive car on the continent.
4. **Mandatory equipment:** warning triangle(s), high-visibility vest(s), first-aid kit (Germany, Austria), fire extinguisher (Italy) — packed and accessible.
5. **Vignette or toll:** purchased and valid for the route (Austria 10-day from ~€9.90; Switzerland annual CHF 40; Czechia 10-day ~€7; Slovakia 10-day ~€7; Hungary 1-week ~€8; Slovenia 7-day ~€7.50).
6. **Speed limits and BAC limits:** noted for each country on the route.
7. **Emergency number:** 112 works everywhere in the EU, free of charge.
