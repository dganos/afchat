# Speed Limits in the EU

## Overview and Legal Framework

Speed limits in the European Union are governed by each member state's national road-traffic law; there is no single EU-wide regulation that mandates specific numeric limits. However, the EU Road Safety Policy Framework and successive Action Plans have encouraged harmonisation toward broadly similar limit tiers: around 50 km/h in built-up areas, 80–100 km/h on rural roads, and 110–130 km/h on motorways. The Vienna Convention on Road Signs and Signals (1968), to which most EU member states are party, sets the baseline for how limits are displayed: white circular signs with a red border show the speed limit in km/h, and the end of a limit zone is marked by the same circle with a diagonal grey or red strike-through, or by "END" text. Speed limits are legally binding from the moment a vehicle enters the zone covered by the sign; ignorance of a sign is not a legal defence in any EU member state.

---

## Default Urban Speed Limits

The standard default speed limit inside built-up areas (localities) across the EU is **50 km/h**. This applies in the absence of a specific sign to the contrary. Notable deviations exist:

- **France**: General urban default is 50 km/h; since July 2018, roads within built-up areas that do not have a centre line are limited to **30 km/h** (instead of 50 km/h) unless signed otherwise. Paris implemented a city-wide 30 km/h zone in August 2021, with only a handful of major boulevards retaining 50 km/h.
- **Netherlands**: The Dutch government lowered the default speed on all urban roads without explicit signage to **30 km/h** effective 1 January 2023 in many municipalities, though the national default remains 50 km/h where 50 km/h signs are posted.
- **Belgium**: Default urban limit is 50 km/h; in the Brussels-Capital Region, a 30 km/h zone (Zone 30) covers most streets since January 2021.
- **Spain**: Default urban limit is **30 km/h** on single-lane roads (in each direction) and 50 km/h on multi-lane urban roads, following a reform effective May 2021.
- **Italy**: 50 km/h within towns; some city centres use 30 km/h zones.

## Default Rural and Open-Road Speed Limits

Outside built-up areas on roads that are not motorways or expressways, limits typically range from **80 to 100 km/h**:

| Country | Rural/Open Road |
|---------|----------------|
| Germany | 100 km/h |
| France | 80 km/h (reduced from 90 km/h in July 2018) |
| Italy | 90 km/h |
| Spain | 90 km/h |
| Netherlands | 80 km/h |
| Poland | 90 km/h (single carriageway); 100 km/h (dual carriageway) |
| Sweden | 80 km/h (default); 100 km/h where signed |
| Austria | 100 km/h |
| Belgium | 70 km/h (Wallonia, Flanders); 70 km/h (Brussels roads leaving city) |
| Portugal | 90 km/h |

France's reduction from 90 km/h to **80 km/h** on two-lane rural roads (Routes nationales and departementales without a central separator) was introduced on **1 July 2018** and remains controversial; individual departments may restore 80 km/h roads to 90 km/h if they can demonstrate a road safety record justifying it.

## Default Motorway Speed Limits

Motorway limits across the EU range from 100 km/h to 140 km/h:

| Country | Motorway Limit (dry) | Motorway Limit (wet/adverse) |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Germany | No general limit (advisory 130 km/h) | Advisory 130 km/h |
| France | 130 km/h | 110 km/h (rain); 110 km/h (visibility < 50 m: 50 km/h) |
| Italy | 130 km/h | 110 km/h (rain or snow) |
| Spain | 120 km/h | 120 km/h (no separate wet limit, but wet-road rules apply) |
| Netherlands | 100 km/h (06:00–19:00); 130 km/h (19:00–06:00) | 100 km/h at all times |
| Poland | 140 km/h | 140 km/h |
| Sweden | 120 km/h | 100 km/h or lower if signed |
| Austria | 130 km/h | 110 km/h |
| Belgium | 120 km/h | 120 km/h |
| Portugal | 120 km/h | 100 km/h |

**Poland** holds the highest legal motorway limit in the EU at **140 km/h** on autostradas. **Germany** is unique in having no mandatory upper speed limit on most Autobahn sections; the **Richtgeschwindigkeit** (advisory limit) of **130 km/h** is not legally binding but carries legal weight if a driver exceeds it and is involved in an accident (the driver may bear partial liability). Approximately 30% of the German Autobahn network carries permanent mandatory limits; the remainder is either unrestricted or has variable electronic signs.

## Country-Specific Notable Variations

**Germany (Autobahn)**: Sections with no mandatory limit are marked by the end-of-limit sign (crossed-out circular sign). Variable Message Signs (VMS) can impose lower limits electronically during congestion, rain, or fog. If a crash occurs while driving faster than 130 km/h on an unrestricted section, contributory negligence is presumed under the advisory-speed doctrine.

**France**: Following the 2018 reduction, France now has one of the lowest rural limits in the EU at 80 km/h. In addition, on motorways, the limit drops from **130 km/h to 110 km/h** when it is raining, and to **50 km/h** when visibility is below 50 metres. A new-driver (licence held under 2 years) motorway limit of **110 km/h** applies in dry conditions and **100 km/h** in rain.

**Netherlands**: The Dutch motorway day/night split — **100 km/h between 06:00 and 19:00, 130 km/h from 19:00 to 06:00** — was introduced in March 2020 primarily to reduce nitrogen-oxide emissions (NOx compliance with EU Natura 2000 obligations), not road safety. This makes the Netherlands unusual in having its strictest motorway limit during daytime.

**Spain**: Following a 2021 reform, urban single-lane roads dropped to 30 km/h as the national default, and roads with two lanes in each direction within towns retained 50 km/h.

## Special Vehicles: Trailers, Buses, and Heavy Lorries

EU member states uniformly apply reduced limits to vehicles towing trailers and to heavy goods vehicles (HGVs). Common limits for **cars towing caravans or trailers** (up to 3.5 t total):

| Country | Urban | Rural | Motorway |
|---------|-------|-------|----------|
| Germany | 50 km/h | 80 km/h | 80 km/h |
| France | 50 km/h | 80 km/h | 90 km/h |
| Italy | 50 km/h | 70 km/h | 100 km/h |
| Spain | 50 km/h | 80 km/h | 80 km/h |
| Netherlands | 50 km/h | 80 km/h | 90 km/h |

**Buses and coaches**: Most EU countries limit buses carrying passengers to 100 km/h on motorways and 80–90 km/h on rural roads. In Germany, buses over 3.5 t gross vehicle weight are limited to **100 km/h** on motorways regardless of the unrestricted sections. In France, coaches are limited to **100 km/h** on motorways (dry) and **90 km/h** in rain.

**Lorries over 3.5 t GVW**: The standard EU-wide expectation (reinforced by the Weights and Dimensions Directive) is that trucks over 3.5 t are limited to **90 km/h** on rural roads and **80–90 km/h** on motorways in most countries. France sets HGV motorway limits at **90 km/h**. Germany limits trucks over 7.5 t to **60 km/h** on rural roads without speed limit signs and to **80 km/h** on motorways. Poland caps HGVs at **90 km/h** on motorways despite the general 140 km/h limit. Many modern HGVs are fitted with mandatory speed limiters capped at **90 km/h** per EU Regulation 92/6/EEC as amended, which effectively enforces the limit for vehicles first registered after 1 January 1988 (coaches) or 1 January 1994 (trucks over 12 t).

## Novice Driver Restrictions

Several EU countries impose reduced limits on drivers who have recently obtained a full licence:

- **France**: Drivers holding a licence for fewer than **2 years** are limited to **110 km/h** on motorways (instead of 130 km/h), **100 km/h** on dual carriageways (instead of 110 km/h or 130 km/h), **80 km/h** on rural roads, and **50 km/h** in towns. A full licence is reached after the 2-year probationary period (or 3 years without accompanied driving).
- **Italy**: During the first **3 years** after obtaining a licence, drivers under 21 years old may not exceed **100 km/h** on motorways and **90 km/h** on non-motorway outside-built-up-area roads.
- **Austria**: Novice drivers (licence held under 2 years, under 21 years of age) are subject to a blood-alcohol limit of 0.1 g/L (zero tolerance effectively) but no separate speed reduction.
- **Ireland**: Learner and novice drivers (N-plate, first 2 years) are restricted to **100 km/h** on motorways where the general limit is 120 km/h.
- **Portugal**: New drivers (first **2 years**) are limited to **90 km/h** on motorways (general limit 120 km/h) and **80 km/h** on rural roads (general limit 90 km/h).

## Adverse Weather and Environmental Reductions

Beyond fixed national limits, many countries mandate or strongly recommend reduced speeds in adverse conditions:

- **Snow and ice**: France, Italy, and Austria require or recommend reducing speed by at least 20 km/h below the normal limit when snow or ice is present. Austria mandates winter tyres (or snow chains) between **1 November and 15 April** on roads where weather conditions require them, and the motorway limit drops to **100 km/h** in these conditions.
- **Fog**: When visibility is below **50 metres**, France mandates a maximum of **50 km/h** regardless of road type. Germany recommends (but does not legally mandate) a speed commensurate with stopping distance within visibility range.
- **Rain**: France reduces the motorway limit from 130 km/h to **110 km/h** at the onset of rain. Italy reduces motorway limits from 130 km/h to **110 km/h** in rain or snow.
- **Variable Message Signs (VMS)**: On heavily instrumented motorways in Germany, the Netherlands, France, and the UK (pre-Brexit), VMS can lower mandatory limits to 60, 80, or 100 km/h during congestion or incidents. Compliance with VMS limits is legally equivalent to fixed-sign limits.

## Minimum Speeds

While most attention focuses on upper limits, some EU motorways carry **minimum speed** requirements to prevent dangerously slow traffic flow. In **Germany**, the Autobahn minimum speed is typically signed at **60 km/h** on sections where such signs are posted; no single national default minimum applies. In **France**, motorways do not have a nationally mandated minimum, but causing obstruction by travelling abnormally slowly can be penalised. In **Poland**, the national road-traffic code specifies a minimum speed of **40 km/h** on motorways and **60 km/h** on expressways during normal conditions. **Italy** sets a minimum of **40 km/h** on Italian autostrade. Minimum speed signs use a blue circular sign with the minimum value in white.

## Signing and Marking

EU member states following the Vienna Convention use a consistent visual language:
- **Speed limit signs**: White disc with red border, black numerals (e.g., "130").
- **End of speed limit**: Same disc struck through diagonally with a grey/red line, or specific "END 130" wording.
- **Zone signs**: Rectangular signs marked "ZONE 30" (or local equivalent) indicate an area where 30 km/h applies to all streets within.
- **Advisory speed signs**: Yellow diamond-shaped signs (Germany) or white rectangular signs convey recommended but non-mandatory speeds.
- **Variable Message Signs**: Green or white overhead matrix signs on motorways; in Germany these are used extensively via ATMS (Adaptive Traffic Management Systems).
- **Road markings**: In some countries, the speed limit may be painted on the road surface in white or yellow, particularly at the entry of lower-speed zones or school areas, as a reminder rather than a legally primary sign.

## Speed Cameras and Enforcement

Enforcement technology varies significantly across EU member states:

**Fixed point cameras**: Installed at permanent locations, typically on high-risk road segments or at traffic signals. Common throughout France, Italy, Spain, and the UK. France operates approximately **4,200 fixed radar speed cameras** as of 2024 (source: French road safety authority).

**Average-speed (section) cameras**: Measure average speed over a measured distance, typically 1–10 km. The UK pioneered SPECS; Italy uses **Autovelox Tutor** systems on autostrade, covering over 1,000 km of motorway. France has deployed **troncon-controle** average-speed sections on dangerous routes nationales. Average-speed systems are considered more effective at sustained compliance than point cameras, since drivers cannot simply brake before the camera.

**Mobile and unmarked cameras**: Used by police in Germany (radar guns), Netherlands (unmarked vans), and Sweden. Germany does not rely heavily on fixed cameras; enforcement is primarily through mobile units.

**Cross-border enforcement**: EU Directive 2015/413/EC (CBE Directive) facilitates exchange of vehicle registration data between member states so that speeding fines can be sent to foreign drivers' home addresses. This covers the 27 EU member states plus Norway, Switzerland, and Iceland.

**Tolerance thresholds**: Most countries add a technical tolerance of **3 km/h** (below 100 km/h) or **3%** (above 100 km/h) before issuing a penalty, though this is an administrative practice, not a legal right, and varies by jurisdiction.
